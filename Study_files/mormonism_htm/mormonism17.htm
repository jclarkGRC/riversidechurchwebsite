Now, some "Do's":

1. Do pray.

We should always be praying for opportunities to witness and for God to use us. We should also pray for those to whom we have spoken that God might powerfully use the words for His glory. If you have made an appointment with a Mormon missionary to come back for a discussion in order to have a chance to witness to him, it is obviously very important to pray for God to prepare his heart to hear the Gospel. We must always remember that such encounters are part of the spiritual warfare that is now raging and only through God’s power will there be victory. Therefore, prayer is primary.

2. Do define your terms clearly.

One of the biggest obstacles to effective communication and witnessing to a Mormon by a Christian is confusion over what is meant by several key terms. It can be frustrating and disconcerting to make several key statements that reflect the true Gospel only to have the Mormon agree with you completely. Some have been fooled into thinking that Mormons really are Christian in their doctrine. Make sure that you and he are both understanding what each of you means by the phrases and key words you discuss.

NOTE: For example, "salvation by grace" which to a Christian refers to a state of righteousness granted to a person through that person’s faith in the finished work of Jesus Christ resulting in an eternal existence in the presence of God in Heaven; but to the Mormon the phrase refers only to a general salvation which is merely physical resurrection granted to all unconditionally. This resurrection is made possible by Christ’s sacrifice which paid for Adam’s sin only, not your personal sin. Thus salvation by grace has nothing to do with your destiny in eternity. You must earn your individual salvation or exaltation by paying for your own sins through good works and complete obedience to the Mormon gospel. Only in that way can you be judged worthy of the third level of the celestial heaven where Heavenly Father dwells.

3. Do ask probing questions.

This is important for at least three reasons. First, it communicates that you have an interest in and a respect for his thoughts and ideas. For most people, it is flattering to be given serious queries. Second, it allows you to draw out what he believes without obnoxiously, presumptuously declaring or implying that you already know everything about his doctrine and his personal beliefs. Third, and perhaps most importantly, leading questions can trigger serious thinking. It is quite easy to disregard another person’s assertions about an issue but it is very difficult to ignore answers that I see for myself when engaging a challenging question. One of our goals is to help the Mormon come to the correct conclusions on his own based on what he can see in the Bible as a result of such questions. We should seek to draw the answers from him by forcing him to consider the matter. This should be done in a calm, non-threatening, unemotional manner. Use your questions to quietly, persistently undermine the authority of the Mormon church and its teachings.

Note: One good area to pursue in this way might be the complete lack of external, historical, archaeological evidence for anything that was recorded in the Book of Mormon. Sometimes the Mormon may defensively testify to you that "Joseph Smith is a prophet of God, the Mormon church is true, Jesus is the Christ, the president of the Mormon church is a prophet on the earth today, and I say this in the name of Jesus Christ, Amen." Ron Rhodes says to reply with, "I testify that Joseph Smith was a false prophet, the Mormon church is not true, Jesus Christ is my personal Lord and Savior, the president of the Mormon church is not a prophet on the earth today, and I say this in the name of Jesus Christ, Amen." (Reasoning from the Scriptures with the Mormons, pp. 34-35). The point to be made is that his testimony is no better than yours. After this exchange (or by other avenues) ask the Mormon what he bases his beliefs on. Most of the time he will talk about the "burning in his bosom." Ask him if he thinks God would expect people to completely base their lives and futures solely on emotional feelings (after all, hasn’t God revealed Himself to us in many ways?). Ask if people ever make drastic mistakes by letting their emotions rule their decisions (suicides, assaults, murders, bad investments?). Ask him if it is a legitimate expectation to see evidence in the real world of what God through his prophets has declared in the Scriptures to be true. If he says "yes" then ask him to show you some evidence that the Book of Mormon accounts are true, like maps of where all the activity took place, etc. (If he refers to Central and South American native ruins you need to be ready to refute that.)

4. Do take your time.

This is the opposite of "Don’t" number two but is worth emphasizing. It is better to thoroughly discuss one or two topics and give him something to chew on than rush through many and cause confusion. If it is the Mormon who is doing the rushing, slow him down. As a corollary, when reading the Scriptures about an issue, read them out loud and have the Mormon focus on what is actually being said. He will often have a pat response for many key Bible passages -- make sure he has the opportunity to consider and comprehend what God’s word is really saying.

Finally, one approach to witnessing to Mormons which is endorsed by ex-Mormon Mark Champneys as being direct, effective and inoffensive involves inquiring about the ultimate goal of following Mormonism. If you ask the thoughtful, serious Mormon this question he will generally reply or agree that the big picture is "to dwell with Heavenly Father." (Don’t use this occasion to attack the idea that achieving this will also allow one to continue on to his godhood and kingdom -- focus on just the idea of getting to be with God the Father in the celestial heaven). But the only problem with this is that no Mormon can have complete assurance that he has done enough. Even after doing all he can do, the final decision will be in the hands of Elohim, Jehovah (Jesus) and Joseph Smith. They can decide that he is not worthy. The upshot is that no Mormon knows for sure that his efforts will be enough. This ultimate insecurity should be troubling to the serious Mormon. Ask the Mormon if he knows for sure he will be in the third level of the celestial heaven. Ask him if he has obeyed completely. Ask him if he has committed the same sin again after requesting forgiveness. Ask him if he has truly stopped sinning altogether. If he is honest he will affirm that he has failed in these things and has no real assurance.

Against this backdrop, the Christian can present the true plan of God’s salvation. He can ask a telling question -- "Why should I trade my full salvation by grace through faith in Jesus with absolute assurance based on God’s Word for a plan in which I must toil endlessly to do good works to offset my sin which I cannot stop committing and which no matter how hard I try I may not measure up?" He may reply that you should trade because he believes his plan is the true one. You should insist, of course, that the evidence does not back that statement up. But no matter what his answer is, you will have given him something to think about. Pray that God will use that doubt and uneasiness to bring him to true understanding. For more on this read Mark Champney’s works listed above.

In conclusion of this section let me encourage us all: Don’t be afraid. Be bold. We are not required to be perfect even in our witnessing -- we are only called to be faithful. While we may never master all the techniques and perhaps never memorize all the Bible verses, we can be sensitive to the leading of the Holy Spirit who can use even poor vessels like us to accomplish His will in another person’s life. My prayer is that this study has helped us a little along the way.
