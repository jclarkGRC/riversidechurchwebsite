II. Beliefs (continued)

F. About the Hereafter

We have already seen that The Latter-day Saints believe that at death a person’s spirit goes either to paradise or a spirit prison to await the day of final judgment. While they are waiting the ones in paradise may share the teachings of Joseph Smith with the ones in prison. The ones in prison may accept these teachings and leave if someone has been baptized on their behalf on earth. In this way they may improve their lot in the final judgment by beginning to work on their own progression (See Reasoning from the Scriptures with the Mormons by Rhodes and Bodine, pp. 351-52.) What we want to consider now is what happens to various groups of people after the day of final judgment. Let’s see what they teach.

The Christian View The Mormon View
1. There is one kingdom of God (heaven). 1. There are three distinct kingdoms.
2. Only true believers in Christ can enter into the kingdom of God. 2. Almost everyone who lives on earth will enter into one of the kingdoms.
3. Those who do not believe in Christ will enter into an eternal place of torment generally referred to as "hell" or the "lake of fire." 3. Spirit prison is temporary for most. The second death (outer darkness) is chiefly for Satan, his followers and the sons of perdition.

** Supporting evidence and quotations of what Mormons really believe:

1. There are three kingdoms or levels of heaven.

a. Doctrine and Covenants 76:89-92-- "And thus we saw, in the heavenly vision, the glory of the telestial, which surpasses all understanding; And no man knows it except him to whom God has revealed it. And thus we saw the glory of the terrestrial which excels in all things the glory of the telestial, even in glory, and in power, and in might, and in dominion. And thus we saw the glory of the celestial, which excels in all things -- where God, even the Father, reigns upon his throne forever and ever;"

FYI: This section of Doctrine and Covenants is an alleged vision given to Joseph Smith and Sidney Rigdon at Hiram, Ohio on February 16, 1832. It is 119 verses long and goes into much detail concerning the three kingdoms and the qualifications required for each. It also reveals the fate of those who are the "sons of perdition."

b. Joseph Smith-- "Except a man be born again, he cannot see the Kingdom of God ... A man may be saved, after the judgment, in the terrestrial kingdom, or in the telestial kingdom, but he can never see the celestial kingdom of God, without being born of water and the Spirit." (History of the Church, vol. 1, p. 283.)

c. LeGrand Richards-- "One of the greatest errors in the teachings of the Christian religions is the doctrine of one heaven and one hell, so that all who go to heaven share and share alike, and all who fail to go to heaven are sent to hell where they share and share alike." (A Marvelous Work and a Wonder, p. 253.)

NOTE: This is not a teaching that most Mormons would avoid or deny even though it is incompatible with biblical teaching. However, they might not want to make this part of an initial discussion with a potential prospect because it is somewhat complicated and confusing compared to the familiar Christian doctrine of one heaven and one hell. Besides the Doctrine and Covenants passage, Mormons like to use I Corinthians 15:40-42 to seek to prove their contention that there is more than one final place of abode after death and the judgment. In the same work referenced above, LeGrand Richards writes: "There is a glory of the sun, or celestial glory; another glory like the moon, or the terrestrial glory; and another glory like the stars, or, as we will learn, the telestial glory; and 'since one star differeth from another star in glory,' so also 'is the resurrection of the dead.' From this we learn that the great multitude in the resurrection will be likened unto the stars in heaven; and just as their works have differed in importance and faithfulness here upon the earth, so also shall their condition in the resurrection differ, even as the stars in heaven differ in glory."

There are a few problems with their interpretation of this passage. Both Rhodes and Bodine in Reasoning from the Scriptures with the Mormons (pp. 379-80) and Jerald and Sandra Tanner in Major Problems of Mormonism (p. 208) point out the following discrepancies: 1) The word "telestial" is not in the passage at all. It is a word made up by Smith and illicitly connected to these verses. Mormons are reading something into the passage that is not there and not even implied; 2) The words "celestial" and "terrestrial" respectively mean "heavenly" and "earthly." Paul was using the words to draw a contrast between that which is earthly (our present existence and experience) and that which is heavenly (the future glory of heaven which we cannot begin to imagine). So once again he is not referring to different kingdoms; 3) The context is made clear in v. 35 in which Paul poses the anticipated questions of "How are the dead raised up? And with what body do they come?" Thus we see that Paul is not talking about different kingdoms but rather about the difference in the sort of body people have now and the heavenly body those who are saved will possess in the future. The latter part of v. 42 drives this home "The body is sown in corruption, it is raised in incorruption."

Another passage used by the Latter-day Saints with regard to their view of heaven is II Corinthians 12:2. They contend that there cannot be a third heaven unless there is a first and second. However, this "logic" is misguided. Jews commonly spoke of three heavens by which they meant: 1) The atmosphere around the earth we usually refer to as the "sky"; 2) The expanse beyond the sky which contains the sun, moon, planets and stars or what is often called "outer space"; 3) The abode of God. To read Mormon theology into Paul’s terminology is to ignore the historical mindset and the common use of language among the Jews.< /font>

2. The three kingdoms will include almost everyone.

a. Joseph Fielding Smith-- "Those who reject the gospel, but live honorable lives, shall also be heirs of salvation, but not in the celestial kingdom. The Lord has prepared a place for them in the terrestrial kingdom. Those who live lives of wickedness may also be heirs of salvation, that is, they too shall be redeemed from death and from hell eventually." (Doctrines of Salvation, vol. 2, p. 133.)

b. John Widtsoe-- "Very few will be so condemned as sons of perdition ... [because] ... very few have the knowledge required. All others, who are not classed as sons of perdition, will be 'redeemed in the due time of the Lord'; that is they will all be saved. The meanest sinner will find some place in the heavenly realm." (Joseph Smith -- Seeker After Truth, p. 177.)

c. Bruce McConkie-- " ... contrary to the views found in the uninspired teachings and creeds of modern Christendom, there are in eternity kingdoms of glory to which all resurrected persons (except the sons of perdition) will eventually go." (Mormon Doctrine, p. 420.)

FYI: The diagram from Major Problems of Mormonism by Jerald and Sandra Tanner provides a good overview of the whole Mormon idea of eternal progression. For our purposes here, consider the three kingdoms and whom they include. Starting at the top we see that the BI kingdom is comprised of Mormons in good standing and children who die before age eight. However, even here there is a difference made in those who were in "complete obedience" and those who did not quite measure up which is demonstrated in this kingdom being itself divided into three parts. While we don’t know exactly what the criteria for the middle level is, the key to entering the top level and thus being eligible for eventual exaltation (godhood and your very own world) seems to be having a marriage which is sealed in a Mormon temple. Those who do not are consigned to the bottom level (or perhaps the middle--who knows?). It is important to note that in Mormon thinking only the top level of the celestial kingdom is regarded as "full salvation." This is the only part of the three kingdoms where a man may continue to be married and produce spirit children, populate his world, etc. This kingdom is where Elohim (the Father) resides and rules.

The terrestrial kingdom mainly consists of people who were "moral" or "honorable" for the most part but did not accept the Mormon faith (such as good Christians and those of other religions but also including those who never heard the "true gospel" of Mormonism). Also those who wait until after death and accept Mormonism in the spirit prison will be here. Finally, some Mormons who were not "valiant" but instead were "lukewarm" may be surprised to find they do not merit the top kingdom but are instead relegated to this level. According to Mark Champneys in Christianity Re-Defined by Mormonism, Jehovah (or Jesus) resides and rules in the terrestrial kingdom (though other sources say Jesus is in the Celestial).

If you are among the vast majority of the world’s wicked population -- you live with no regard for what’s right, good and kind, neither do you fear God but rather only indulge your selfish, carnal appetites -- you will end up in the telestial kingdom. Those who are destined for this lowest level will probably have to endure some time in a place of torment to make up for their evil conduct. Champneys comments that the Holy Spirit resides and rules here in a place that is "still far more wonderful than earth" (to say nothing of what hell would be like).

The bottom line is that almost everyone will find a place in one of the three kingdoms. We might say that the average Mormon believes in a universal salvation for all men. Indeed, they themselves say this as R.H. Evans who proclaimed, "Mormons believe in universal salvation that all men will be saved, but each to his own order." (Look magazine, Oct. 5, 1954.) Thus, while only the cream of the crop will attain to the top level of the celestial kingdom, almost everyone else will receive a pleasant existence as a servant in one of the lesser kingdoms (though sometimes these who are in lesser, serving roles are said by Mormons to be "damned," especially those in the telestial kingdom).

Mormons like to belittle the fact that Christians teach that all believers will inherit the same kingdom. They argue against the idea that all should receive the same inheritance when some are much more faithful than others. This is a misconception based on an incomplete knowledge of what most Christians believe. Walter Martin (Kingdom of the Cults, p. 217) points out that indeed we declare that all genuine believers will enter into the one kingdom of God which is the "base pay" described in the parable of the laborers in Matthew 20:1-16. Eternal life in God’s kingdom will be received by all who genuinely believe no matter at what time of life or the amount of work they do for the Lord. Martin points to Jesus’ promise to the thief on the cross next to Him as evidence of that principle (Luke 23:42-43). Other scripture passages to consider which teach that there is only one kingdom where all believers go to be with the Lord are: Matthew 25:29; John 12:25-26; I Thessalonians 2:12; Hebrews 12:28; James 2:5. Besides these there are a multitude of references to both the "kingdom of God" and the "kingdom of heaven." These references are always singular, never plural. There is no support for multiple kingdoms in the Bible. Incidentally, once more as in other cases, their own Book of Mormon does not support the idea either. Nowhere in their primary source of authority is this doctrine taught. The "later vision" recorded in Doctrine and Covenants 76 is the sole basis for this belief.

With regard to those who are longtime, faithful servants versus those who are not as faithful, Mormons fail to consider the biblical teaching of rewards in heaven. In I Corinthians 3:11-15 and II Corinthians 5:10 Paul teaches that believers will be judged according to how they have served and will be rewarded accordingly. Jesus also in His parable of the talents (Matthew 25:14-29) and of the minas (Luke 19:11-24) seems to be teaching that His people will be given rewards and responsibilities according to their service and faithfulness while waiting for His return. The Bible also teaches the concept of degrees of punishment in hell (cf. Matthew 10:15; 11:21-24; Luke 12:47-48), which brings us to the last point.

3. There is no eternal hell for most.

a. James Talmage-- "No soul shall be kept in prison or continued in torment beyond the time requisite to work the needed reformation and vindicate justice for which ends alone punishment is imposed. After the debt has been paid the prison doors shall be opened, and the spirits once confined in suffering, then chastened and clean, shall come forth to partake of the glory provided in their class." (A Study of the Articles of Faith, pp. 146-48.)

b. John Widtsoe-- "How men could devise so horrible a future for any one of God’s children is a striking evidence of the apostasy from the simple loving gospel of Jesus Christ. ... The correction of this evil doctrine had to be made. ... In the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, there is no hell. All will find a measure of salvation. ... The gospel of Jesus Christ has no hell in the old proverbial sense." (Joseph Smith -- Seeker After Truth, pp. 175, 177-78.)

NOTE: By now it is plain that when a Latter-day Saint talks about hell he does not usually mean the same thing a Christian does. As the quotes above and previous discussion reveal, one Mormon idea is that men who believed not the teachings of Joseph Smith go to a spirit prison where they suffer until they either accept Mormonism and someone is baptized for them on earth or apparently they simply suffer enough to merit release at the judgment or at some point afterward. The other main idea of hell seems to be that of the second death or outer darkness. This is a place of eternal suffering but it is limited to only Satan and his followers in the original spirit world who rebelled against Elohim and then the sons of perdition. In his pamphlet Christianity Re-Defined by Mormonism, Mark Champneys concisely defines the Mormon meaning of hell and reveals who these sons of perdition are mainly thought to be: "There is no hell per se. If hell is mentioned it is a reference to (1) the Telestial Kingdom; (2) Spirit Prison, a temporary destination before judgment; or (3) Outer Darkness, a place reserved for the devil and the demons (and Ex-Mormons)." (Champneys is himself is an ex-Mormon who was once very active in the organization.)

There are, of course, some problems with this denial of eternal punishment by the Latter-day Saints. First, once again, it is inconsistent with their main source of authority, the Book of Mormon. It seems Joseph Smith was at first in strong disagreement with those referred to in his day as universalists who taught that a loving, just God would not punish men endlessly. (See Major Problems of Mormonism, pp. 212-13, by Tanner and Tanner for more information on this.) This early position by Joseph Smith is reflected in the Book of Mormon, cf. 2 Nephi 28:19-23; Mosiah 2:38-39; Alma 42:16; 3 Nephi 27:11, 17. However, not long after the publication of the Book of Mormon, Smith reversed his position and denied in various publications and in the Doctrine and Covenants that there will be such a thing as endless or eternal punishment (cf. Section 19:6-7, 10-12). More confusion in this convoluted and false system of thought.

Another perplexing issue for the Mormon is the final outcome in the one place of real eternal punishment they do endorse. The Tanners in their previously referenced work (pp. 214-216 in a section entitled An Ever- Expanding Hell) do a great job exposing this conundrum. No doubt the intention of the LDS theology by restricting the Second Death or Outer Darkness to only Satan, the demons and the sons of perdition is to make the idea of such a place more palatable. If anyone deserves such treatment surely it is these -- and besides, it’s not many, right? Not so fast say the Tanners. In Doctrines and Covenants 29, Smith reveals that a third of all the spirit children followed Satan. Mormon writer Eugene England estimates that by the end of time eventually 80 billion people will have lived on the earth. Remember that this represents pretty much all the spirit children who did not go after Satan. Those who did follow Lucifer do not get to have earthly lives. If these made up a third of the total then doing the math reveals that Satan was followed by about 40 billion spirit children. Remember these are the spirit children of Elohim! In his quotation above, while John Widtsoe belittles the biblical teaching of eternal punishment for the wicked with his comment about "such a horrible future for any one of God’s children", his own theology blithely condemns 40,000,000,000 of God’s children to that place. That does not even include the ones who apostasize from the LDS church whom most Mormons believe will join Satan in the second death because they had the "truth" and rejected it. But it gets worse. Remember that this process has been repeated and will be repeated an infinite number of times. Brigham Young comments, "How many earths are there? I observed this morning that you may take the particles of matter composing this earth, and if they could be enumerated they would only be a beginning to the number of creations of God; and they are continually coming into existence, and undergoing changes and passing through the same experience that we are passing through. Sin is upon every earth that ever was created, ... Consequently every earth has its redeemer, and every earth has its tempter; and every earth, and the people thereof, in their turn and time, receive all that we receive, and pass through all the ordeals that we are passing through.” (Journal of Discourses, vol. 14, pp. 71-72.) So according to one of their greatest leaders and prophets this process is duplicated at an astronomical rate. It is not unreasonable to expect that each "god" should lose about the same number of children each time a tempter arises so what we are faced with is ultimately quadrillions of creations with billions from each one lost to the outer darkness of eternal suffering. If the Christian doctrine of hell is "horrible," as John Widtsoe (quoted above) asserted, is the LDS position any better?

Of course, the bottom line problem with the Mormon concept of no hell for almost everyone who ever lived on earth is that the Bible disagrees with it. Earlier we refuted the LDS attempt to wrangle three kingdoms out of the Bible but we never presented what the Bible teaches about what the potential destinies of man are. So let’s return and see what God’s word says about that. Rhodes and Bodine point out that anytime the Bible speaks about judgment and the afterlife it always refers to two classes of people and two possible destinies (Reasoning from the Scriptures with the Mormons, pp. 373-75). Note the following passages: Daniel 12:2; Matthew 13:24-30, 37-43; Matthew 25:31-46; Luke 16:19-26. The only two classes are those who are deemed as righteous (because of their faith in the Lord) and those judged to be wicked (because of their unbelief and sin). The only possible final destinations are the kingdom of God (heaven) and the lake of fire (hell).

Note that, contrary to Mr. Widtsoe's comments about the doctrine of hell being contrary to the "simple loving gospel of Jesus Christ," in most of the passages we saw it was Jesus Himself doing the teaching. There are many more. Jesus often spoke about the coming judgment and warned of the irreversible, forever nature of God’s condemnation. The Bible paints a very vivid picture and insists on the reality of a place of burning, fiery torment. In the last book, Revelation, it is quite clear what the stakes are with regard to our accepting or rejecting Jesus Christ (see Revelation 14:10-11; 19:19-20; 20:10, 15; 21:8).

While, admittedly, hell is not a pleasant subject, it is worse than irresponsible to deny that such a place exists and fail to warn those around us about what’s ahead for those who do not commit their lives to the Lord. In fact, the Bible says that those who fail in this will be accountable for the "blood on their hands" of those they should have warned (cf. Ezekiel 33:1-9). While we understand the natural desire to not want to dwell on negative things or to hope that bad news will not come to pass, we dare not ignore or deny the warnings of Scripture. We must not sacrifice the truth for the sake of blissful ignorance. We must not let people perish for the sake of avoiding controversial issues.
