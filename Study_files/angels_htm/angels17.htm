A Brief Study of Angels

Where does Satan come from?

There is not a definitive answer to this question. In the Scriptures Satan appears on the scene suddenly in Genesis 3 either disguised as or, perhaps more likely, in possession of the serpent which spoke to Eve. We know this serpent was Satan because of Revelation 12:9; 20:2. But how did Satan get there to begin with? We cannot be certain, but we can make some logical deductions. To start, we know that the Lord God created everything (cf. Gen. 1:1; Col. 1:16ff). So Satan was originally created by God. But this raises another question--if God created everything "good" in Genesis 1 (and because of God's perfect holiness, we know that He would not create anything that was "bad" on this or any occasion) then why is Satan so evil? We can thus assume that Satan was not created in his current state of evil but rather fell from a state of holiness and service to God through sinning (much like Adam who chose to disobey God and also fell from his perfect state).

After hearing these things, another question might be: “Okay then, so what was Satan created as before he fell?” The majority of Bible scholars would answer, "He was originally a holy angel." Why do they think this? First, as we saw in the section on demons, there are several passages that connect Satan and the fallen angels (demons)--cf. Matthew 12:24-26; 25:41; Luke 10:17-20; Revelation 12:7-9. It makes sense, then, that Satan is also an evil angel, but the one who is the leader.

Second, Paul makes a reference to the devil who “transforms himself into an angel of light” (cf. II Cor. 11:14). Some take this as a statement that points toward Satan’s original nature. The context is that Paul is comparing the tactics of false apostles to those of Satan who inspires them. They seek to deceive others into leaving the pure gospel and that is how the devil operates as well. But Paul’s main point concerns Satan’s mode of operation, not his essential nature. However, one could argue that these fake apostles were passing themselves off as disciples of Christ which was consistent with their nature as men. Just so, Satan’s passing himself off as a holy angel of God would be consistent with his nature as an angelic spirit being. Plausible but nevertheless inconclusive reasoning. For a verse which may more clearly call Satan an angel, a better one might be found in Revelation 9:11 which most understand to refer to the devil.

The third passage which we will refer to concerning his original nature may be the most informative but is also controversial. It is found in Ezekiel 28 and some deny that it is speaking about Satan at all. If it does not, then we have little to go on about Satan’s beginnings. However, there are good reasons to believe it does speak of the devil and many good conservative scholars affirm this. Ezekiel primarily prophesied to the captive Jews in Babylon in the sixth century B.C. In chapters 25-32 he shared God’s promise of judgment on various nations for their wickedness. In chapter 28 verses 1-10 the focus is on the “prince of Tyre” which Robert Lightner tells us refers to Ethbaal III who was an arrogant, wicked man (Angels, Satan, and Demons, p. 69). But in v. 12 Ezekiel speaks of another who is said to be the “king of Tyre.” Here is where the disagreement starts. Some believe this is simply a continuation of the prophecy against Ethbaal. Others think it marks a change of subject and through v. 19 refers to Satan as he was before his fall and immediately after. Consider the following. For one thing, the first set of verses specifically say that the subject (“the prince”) was a “man” (v. 2). The second part of the passage says that the subject (“the king”) was “the anointed cherub who covers” (vv. 14, 16). And that’s not all--it describes this being as “the seal of perfection” (v. 12) and “perfect in your ways” (v. 15) which has not been true of any man after Adam. He is said to have been in “Eden, the garden of God” (v. 13) and “on the holy mountain of God” (v. 14--perhaps meaning in God's presence). Twice he is said to have been “created” (vv. 13, 15). From verse 15 onward we are told of his “iniquity” and subsequent judgment. From these statements and descriptions it seems reasonable to suggest that the passage portrays a being beyond the normal human experience. It makes logical sense to believe that it describes Satan as originally being one of the highly privileged angels who was appointed to serve “as a protector or honor guard of God’s presence and holiness” (Angels, Satan, and Demons, p. 69). Some even assert that before his fall he was the greatest or highest in rank of all the angels created. Though we cannot be dogmatic, it makes sense that an angel near or at the top of the original hierarchy of angelic beings would be the one to lead others in a rebellion against the Lord.

One final consideration of this question about the origin of Satan involves what he actually did to fall. Ezekiel gives us a hint in 28:17 when he says that Satan’s “heart was lifted up.” Another controversial passage (for the same reason--not all agree it speaks of Satan) which may provide more detail is Isaiah 14:12-15. In the context Isaiah prophesies against the king of Babylon (vv. 3-11). There are several interpretations to explain v. 12ff. and we admit it is easier to rectify this passage being attributed to a human king than Ezekiel’s. Still, many think that the name of the subject “Lucifer” (or “Day Star”), “son of the morning” (“shining one”) and the fact that he was “fallen from heaven” (cf. Luke 10:18) point to a description of Satan’s fall. Also, the five “I will” statements of vv. 13-14 seem to be beyond what a mere man (even a king) could aspire to and fit more with Satan's desires. If this is speaking of Satan’s sin then it is clear that he foolishly (Ezek. 28:17) acted in pride to attempt to unseat God and take His place. This resulted in his fall from holiness, being cast out and becoming the being who we know today as the devil, first and ultimate enemy of God.
