Question 21: Will Heaven really be as great as it is thought to be or could it become tedious?"

To demonstrate what some think about what Heaven will be like, Randy Alcorn cites well-known science fiction author Isaac Asimov who wrote:

I don't believe in the afterlife, so I don't have to spend my whole life fearing hell, or fearing heaven even more. For whatever the tortures of hell, I think the boredom of heaven would be even worse. (Heaven, p. 393.)
In this session we want to explore the reasoning behind such a statement. Why do some (possibly even some Christians) picture Heaven from that perspective? There are several possible answers, all of which may contribute in some degree to this erroneous attitude:
  1. Unlimited time may be very frightening for those whose lives have no real purpose. I have suggested that, for me anyway, one of the amazing benefits I see regarding heaven is not having to worry about the passing or shortage of time. For myself, it seems I am continuously up against a deadline to have something prepared, or to be somewhere, or to get something done and the thought of not having to stress about that sounds very good. But for someone who is trying to fill his time but has nothing much to keep him occupied, it is easy to see why the thought of eternal life might seem unpleasant or even terrifying. In the movie The Green Mile the main character is granted an enormously extended lifespan. We eventually find out that for him it only meant that he had to continuously watch those around him die while he kept on living. It actually discouraged him from seeking relationships because he knew it would only lead to pain.

  2. Fun and excitement is readily associated with sin or evil or "naughtiness," while "being good" or even God Himself is related to boredom. Alcorn comments:

    "Won't it boring to be good all the time?" someone asked [me]. Note the assumption: sin is exciting and righteousness is boring. We've fallen for the devil's lie. His most basic strategy, the same one he employed with Adam and Eve, is to make us believe that sin brings fulfillment ...

    An elderly gentleman I led to Christ asked a question of a Christian employee in his care center: "Will we have fun in Heaven?" "Oh, no!" the woman replied, appearing dismayed that he'd even asked. When he told me this story, I shook my head, because I've heard it so often. Why did this Christian woman respond as she did? Because in accordance with the faulty assumptions of Christoplatonism, she instinctively linked fun with sin and boredom with holiness. (Heaven, pp. 394-95)

    This lie is reinforced by the fact that many carnal activities which are forbidden in God's Word do provide the quick buzz, or sudden intense pleasure (cf. Hebrews 11:25). So because things like gluttony, drunkenness, fornication, etc. are condemned, people mistakenly conclude that God is against good times and fun. Thus Heaven will be a terribly boring and stifling place.

  3. Since everything is "perfect" in Heaven it is not possible that there can be any new challenges. People rightly understand that much satisfaction comes from solving problems, overcoming obstacles, improving one's skills and personal growth. But they wrongly assume that in Heaven these things are absent -- Alcorn comments:
    Someone told me nobody will enjoy playing golf in Heaven because it would get boring always hitting holes in one.
  4. Some professing believers think Heaven will be boring based on the fact that their own Christian experience is drab and unexciting. But whose fault is that? It is often the case that two people going through the same experience may have very different responses to that experience based solely on what they themselves bring to it. People often think that Christianity (even their own) is boring. This may be due to the fact that they think (have been taught) that to be a Christian one has to try to be "good" all the time. Thus they spend their time and effort being "nice". Don't misunderstand -- being "nice" and "good" are proper for general Christian demeanor, but there is much more to it than that! Was Jesus "nice" all the time (cf. Matthew 21:12-13)?
Uh-oh. Now we're starting to respond to the reasoning behind the thinking that Heaven will be boring.

While addressing this next-to-last question I want to review and sort of consolidate some thoughts we have discussed at several different points in the study.

We have examined four possible reasons people sometimes use to defend the idea that Heaven will be unbearably boring. There may be other reasons and excuses people give to try to explain why they are ambivalent about Heaven, but these seem to cover most of the variations. Are they valid? Now that we have gone through this study you should be able to answer most of the concerns. But let's go through them and offer a response to each.

The first assumption, thinking unlimited time will be impossible to fill up with interesting and fulfilling activity, implies the residents of the New Earth won't be busy or have purpose. We have seen the purpose of redemption and the restoration of all things is to make all things right. Part of that is that we are free to become what we were made and gifted to be. This means our activities and occupations will be satisfying, worthwhile and enjoyable. Also Jesus teaches that there will be responsibilities in Heaven -- cf. Luke 19:17. It's not just lollygagging in the clouds practicing on harps (where did that image come from anyway?). And besides all that, who says we will be limited to this planet to pass the time? Yes I think the New Earth will be "home sweet home" but why create such an enormous universe if God did not intend for it to be explored? If we are to have dominion over all His creation we'll need to get out there and have a look at it. Yes, that seems a huge undertaking since space is infinite. Just the thing, then, to fill up and occupy the "excessive" amount of time we'll have.

We saw that the second assumption that sin and evil bring fun and fulfillment and God and goodness are boring is a lie from Satan. Randy Alcorn speaks well to this:

People sometimes say, "I'd rather be having a good time in hell than be bored in Heaven." Many people imagine Hell as a place where they'll hang around, shoot pool, and joke with friends. That could happen on the New Earth, but not in Hell.

Hell is a place of torment and isolation, where friendship and good times don't exist. Hell will be deadly boring. Everything good, enjoyable, refreshing, fascinating, and interesting is derived from God. Without God there's nothing interesting to do. ...

Our belief that Heaven will be boring betrays a heresy -- that God is boring. There's no greater nonsense. Our desire for pleasure and the experience of joy comes directly from God's hand. He made our taste buds, adrenaline, sex drives, and the nerve endings that convey pleasure to our brains. Likewise our imaginations and our capacity for joy and exhilaration were made by the very God we accuse of being boring. Are we so arrogant as to imagine that human beings came up with the idea of having fun? ...

Those who believe that excitement can't exist without sin are thinking with sin-poisoned minds. Drug addicts are convinced that without drugs they can't live happy lives. In fact -- as everyone else can see -- drugs make them miserable. Freedom from sin will mean freedom to be what God intended, freedom to find far greater joy in everything. (Heaven, pp. 393-94.)

The third assumption that "perfection" means all challenges and obstacles are removed (in other words we never make a mistake or fall short of a goal) is not based in Scripture. The idea of perfection in Heaven relates primarily to moral perfection -- the absence of sin and evil. But that does not mean the Lord will remove all failure from His kingdom. What is it that strengthens and stretches us? What is maybe the most satisfying experience we can have personally? Perhaps it is when we have faced a daunting task or test and, having failed, come back and overcome it later. We have seen that people need to grow and develop and learn. It is part of what makes us human. It does not seem reasonable the Lord would eliminate the aspects which stimulate such growth from the New Earth.

The fourth and final reason speaks more directly to believers whose lives of faith are not very exciting to them. Again let me quote Alcorn:

Another reason people assume Heaven is boring is that their Christian lives are boring. That's not God's fault; it's their own. God calls us to follow him in an adventure that should put us on life's edge. He's infinite in creativity, goodness, beauty, and power. If we're experiencing the invigorating stirrings of God's Spirit, trusting him to fill our lives with divine appointments, experiencing the childlike delights of his gracious daily kindnesses, then we'll know that God is exciting and Heaven is exhilarating. (Heaven, p. 395.)

As we come to the very last question of this study we are going to think about some of the conclusions we can draw about what we have learned and discussed.
