Pertinent Questions - 3

In our first pertinent question segment we considered the nature of the spiritual gift of speaking in tongues, or in other words, what was it exactly? We saw that there is only one passage (Acts 2) which actually describes what was happening as the gift was being exercised. There it is plain that what was being spoken were human languages. Since the same Greek words for 'speak' and 'tongue' are used in the other four passages which predict, record or address the use of tongues it would seem most reasonable to conclude that the gift of tongues was the same throughout. This is a critical determination because a large point of contention between charismatics and non-charismatics is how charismatics redefine tongues as either a heavenly language or a private prayer language. I would submit there is no biblical warrant for doing that The one trying to make such a distinction between Acts 2 and I Corinthians 14 must shoulder the burden of proof.

If we accept that the spiritual gift of tongues was always the miraculous ability to speak in a language previously unknown to the speaker, we can turn to the next logical question. What was the purpose of the gift? In other words, why did the Holy Spirit settle on this particular manifestation of supernatural power? Or more to the point perhaps, what was it really good for? As we have been reading in I Corinthians 12-14 it seems to have created more problems than it solved in the church. I think it is a fair statement to say that the same is true today. But back to the question. What was the primary purpose of speaking in tongues?

It has been suggested by some that God had a very practical purpose for granting to some the ability to speak in languages they did not know. Indeed some might say that Acts 2:7-11 paints a very clear picture. It seems obvious that God's heart is for evangelism and He gave these Galileans the ability to speak in foreign languages so those from other countries could hear the gospel. But is this so? Certainly the Lord received glory from this event and later in the chapter some three thousand are saved. Nevertheless I would submit that this idea about evangelism being the primary purpose of tongues has some fairly sizable flaws.

First, the idea that the listeners in this passage from those other nations needed this miracle to be able to comprehend and understand the message is clearly wrong. It is almost certain that all the people present during this event had, not one, but two languages in common with each other. The common trade language was, of course, Greek and most people had an understanding of it. That all the original and early New Testament manuscripts were written in koine' Greek underscores this truth. Beyond that we see in v. 5, 10 that the listeners we are discussing are Jews and proselytes. So most, if not all, of them would also be able to speak in Aramaic, a form of Hebrew. Need more proof about this idea of these foreigners already having a common language? Did you note in v. 7 that these men from the various nations were able to speak to each other about what was happening right there before their eyes (or, rather, right there into their ears)?

Consider also the fact that in v. 14 Peter begins to preach to the entire crowd and it wasn't until he finished that 3000 were saved. Are we to suppose that his message was somehow in more than one language? I guess it is possible that he spoke and then various ones interpreted into foreign languages as needed. Or maybe Peter spoke and the listeners miraculously heard his words in their own language. Indeed, some have supposed that this was really what was happening the whole time. We have already addressed this but let me once again quote Robert Picirilli:

Some interpreters attempt to make this a miracle of hearing, saying the people heard in languages not being spoken at all. But surely the phrase "in our tongues" (2:11) modifies "speak," not "hear." Likewise 2:6. I doubt that such a strange interpretation of the record would ever have been thought of had there not been a need to justify some kind of "tongues" other than real languages. (What the Bible Says about Tongues, p. 7.)

I also commented at that point that: "If it was a miracle of hearing, and not of speaking, then why didn't all the men present understand (cf. Acts 2:13)? And I might also add that it is called the gift of 'tongues,' not the gift of 'ears.'"

The point is that while there are other possibilities involved in this matter, the simplest and most straightforward conclusion is that though the Galileans did speak in all these different languages, and the Jews from other places understood them, yet they all had at least one common language with which they could and did communicate. This truth at the very least undermines the idea that the main purpose of speaking in tongues was to evangelize those who did not speak the language of the one witnessing to them.

Consider one other thing. If we allow (for the sake of argument) that the true gift of tongues continues today, and if evangelism is the main purpose, then why do missionaries have to spend so much time in language school? It would seem that if the Holy Spirit was going to give anyone such a gift it would be those who are called to go to other places in the world to share the gospel. Unless, of course, breaking down language barriers for evangelism wasn't the primary reason God granted this gift? I submit that it was not.

Then what was the purpose? We do not have to guess. Two of our five passages tell us that it was given for a 'sign.' But a sign of what? That's for the next segment.
