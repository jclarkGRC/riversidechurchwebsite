Biblical Passages - 1

For all the discussion about 'speaking in tongues' these days, the references to the phenomenon in the New Testament are surprisingly limited. Citing W. Graham Scroggie, Ronald Baxter shares the following breakdown:

  1. There is one reference only to tongues in the four Gospels.
  2. There are three distinct references to tongues in the book of Acts.
  3. There is reference to tongues in only one of the twenty-one epistles of the New Testament... (Charismatic Gift of Tongues, p.1.)

It is from these passages, then, that we must make whatever discoveries and conclusions we can. Let us proceed.

The first mention of tongues in the New Testament is in Mark 16:17. It is best to read the entire passage from vv. 14-20 so we can see the full context. This particular text is not used very much by writers in treatments of the spiritual gift of tongues for possibly two reasons: 1) Because the text here is controversial to some. There have been disputes about whether this portion of chapter 16 was original with Mark or added later based on evidence from early manuscripts (or, rather, the lack of it). Some do not care to base their views of doctrine on such a passage; 2) More pertinent to us, there is not a lot of information here about tongues except a prophecy by Jesus that they would appear.

However, I do think there are some things that we can find here both plainly and linguistically which will be useful in laying a foundation for the other passages. Let's start with some of the Greek words. The root for 'tongues' is glossa. This word is used in only three different ways in the New Testament. In a pamphlet entitled Tongues...in the Light of the Bible, E.L. Bynum mentions that the word "Glossa" is found 50 times in the New Testament and is translated 'tongues' all 50 times. Sixteen times it refers to the tongue as an organ of the body. One time it refers to the tongues "like as of fire" on the day of Pentecost. In the other 33 places it refers to languages as in Revelation 10:11, "... before many peoples, and nations, and tongues..." (p. 3). So we see that there need not be a mystical connotation to the word itself. It was simply the Greek word used to refer to the tongue in your mouth or the language coming out of it.

Likewise the word for 'speak' (root laleo) is just the normal Greek expression for someone saying something. This, too, is the usual word utilized when we see the phenomenon appear in the Scriptures. However, the word for 'new' in this passage (root kainos) is not so common in this particular usage. In fact it is not used at all elsewhere as a modifier for 'tongues' (we will see the other adjectives later in those passages). What is the meaning of 'new' in this instance? To clarify, does it refer to something never ever seen before at all or does it mean that something is new to those involved in a certain situation? W.E. Vine, in his Expository Dictionary of Biblical Words, comments that the word

denotes 'new,' of that which is unaccustomed or unused, not 'new' in time, recent, but 'new' as to form or quality, of different nature from what is contrasted as old. 'The new tongues,' kainos, of Mark 16:17 are the 'other tongues', heteros, of Acts 2:4. These languages, however, were 'new' and 'different,' not in the sense that they had never been heard before, or that they were new to the hearers, for it is plain from v. 8 that this is not the case; they were new languages to the speakers, different from those in which they were accustomed to speak. (p.431)

Thus, at least here, it seems that 'new' does not equate to 'previously unknown.'

Now let's turn to a few simple observations which I think will help set the tone, so to speak, for what we will see later in Acts and I Corinthians. The first item to note is Jesus' clear indication that this 'speak[ing] with new tongues' (along with the other miraculous activities mentioned) are for 'signs [which] will follow those who believe' (Mark 16:17). This is consistent with the other passages we will see and, really, no one disputes that this is the case. The real question, for our purposes, (and which we will consider later) is for how long do the sign gifts follow believers?

The second item of note is in Mark 16:20 which confirms that all this which Jesus predicted actually happened. In fact the Book of Acts records some very specific examples of such occurrences. Remember that, even though Mark was very likely the earliest gospel account written, it was not composed until around AD 58. Thus, by that time, the Church had been active and moving for almost 30 years after Jesus' crucifixion and resurrection.

What's the point? Well, from one point of view, it could be inferred that since the prophecy was fulfilled then it means that such signs are not for today or, at least they are not necessary to fulfill Jesus' prophecy. Of course, the opposing view might respond by saying that just because we see that it happened in those early days does not mean it cannot continue to happen on into the future and even to today. What I want to establish is that this passage cannot be used to conclusively prove that tongues are for today or that they are not. And, of course, that is why the argument is not absolutely settled in the Christian community because either view could be true.

And, in fact, we will see that this is the case for all the passages we will examine. There is no place where the Holy Spirit inspired a writer to explicitly tell us the shelf life for His gifts to the Church at large. But I do believe there is a preponderance of evidence to be found in the honest exposition of the Scriptures and from that information a person can make a good and sound decision about 'tongues.'
