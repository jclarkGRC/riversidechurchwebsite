Pertinent Questions - 20

We are coming rapidly to the close (I think) of our study about the modern practice of speaking in tongues in Christian circles. We have covered a lot of ground. We have reviewed the history of this sort of tongues-speaking and have tried to look at all the pertinent passages with a view to considering the context and what the verses are actually trying to communicate. Finally we have tried to answer specific pertinent questions which I have tried to formulate based on what seem to be most people's concerns about this issue.

The last pertinent question (again...I think) is: Is there any real harm in the modern practice of speaking in tongues? In other words, what real difference does it make if a group of Christians or if an individual believes that the Holy Spirit still gives out this sign gift? So what if they decide to make ecstatic speaking part of their worship? Does that mean that they are not true believers or have forfeited their salvation? If not, then what's the problem with something that makes them feel good or more spiritual (i.e., it edifies them)? Are we cessationists who create studies or write papers or books critiquing this practice causing unnecessary division? Are we troublemakers? These are fair questions which deserve an answer.

First, let me say (again) that I do not believe that if a professing Christian practices speaking in tongues (whether publicly in a church service or privately in prayer) that this in itself disqualifies him or her from being a born again Christian. I do vehemently disagree with the person's doctrinal position on the matter but the same could be said of a multitude of various issues with other denominations and theologies. The acid test of someone's salvation is not a particular doctrinal stance but it is, rather, the possession of true faith in the Lord Jesus Christ. This faith should create a hunger and obedience for biblical truth which should, in turn, cause most believers to resemble each other closely (especially as compared with unbelievers6) but there is room for honest disagreement in nonessential biblical teachings. John MacArthur is to the point in his Charismatic Chaos (pp. 298-99) as he writes:

"We can thank God for charismatic and Pentecostal people who believe in the Word of God. We can be grateful that they believe the Bible and hold it up as authoritative, though we are concerned about their view of revelation. We can also praise God that they believe in the deity of Jesus Christ, his sacrificial death, his physical resurrection, salvation by faith not works, and the need to live in obedience to Christ, fervently love their fellow man, and proclaim the faith with zeal."

He then follows that statement with one that addresses the question of whether studies and writings like this create needless controversy:

"Some might say, 'Why criticize them?' We do so because it is scriptural to be concerned about whether our brothers and sisters are walking in the truth. Although it may not seem very loving to some, the Bible is clear that we are to 'speak the truth in love' (Eph. 4:15). True love must act on the truth."

And there is more to it than just a difference of doctrinal opinion. The reason good doctrine is important in the first place is that it eventually shows up in real life situations. In other words there are consequences when a person misinterprets the Bible and goes in a direction that is not according to God's will. Once more let me refer to MacArthur's book as he cites three men who were all once involved with the charismatic tongues movement (Ibid., pp. 273-74). The first is the late George Gardiner, a pastor, who wrote:

"The enemy of the soul is ever ready to take an advantage of an 'out of control' situation and thousands of Christians can testify with regret to the end results. Such experiences not only give Satan an opening he is quick to exploit, they can be psychologically damaging to the individual. Charismatic writers are constantly warning tongues-speakers that they will suffer a letdown. This is ascribed to the devil and the reader is urged to get refilled as soon as possible. ... So the seeker for experience goes back through the ritual again and again, but begins to discover something: ecstatic experience, like drug addiction, requires larger and larger doses to satisfy. Sometimes the bizarre is introduced. I have seen people run around a room until they were exhausted, climb tent poles, laugh hysterically, go into trances for days and do other weird things as the 'high' sought became more elusive. Eventually there is a crisis and a decision is made; he will sit on the back seats and be a spectator, 'fake it,' or go on in the hope that everything will eventually be as it was. The most tragic decision is to quit and in the quitting abandon all things spiritual as fraudulent. The spectators are frustrated, the fakers suffer guilt, the hoping are pitiable and the quitters are a tragedy. No, such movements are not harmless!"

Another testimony comes from Wayne Robinson, once on the staff of the Oral Roberts Evangelistic Association, who penned:

"In the past few years I have become more and more convinced that the [emphasis his] test, not only of tongues but of any religious experience cannot be limited to the logic and truthfulness supporting it. There is also the essential question, 'What does it do in one's life?' More specifically, does it turn a person inward to self-concern and selfish interests, or does it open him up to others and their needs? I know people who testify that speaking in tongues has been the great liberating experience of their lives. But juxtaposed with them are a great many others for whom speaking in tongues has been an excuse to withdraw from confronting the realities of a suffering and divided world. For some, tongues has been the greatest thing ever to happen; others have seen it disrupt churches, destroy careers, and rupture personal relationships."

Finally, former charismatic Ben Byrd writes:

"To say that speaking in tongues is a harmless practice and is all right for those who want to IS AN UNWISE [emphasis his] position when information to the contrary is evident. ... Speaking in tongues is addictive. The misunderstanding of the issue of tongues and the habit plus the psychic high it brings plus the stimulation of the flesh equals a practice hard to let go of. ... [But to] equate much speaking in tongues with advanced spirituality is to reveal one's misunderstanding of Bible Truth and to reveal one's willingness to be satisfied with a deceptive and dangerous counterfeit."

We will conclude this question and summarize in the final segment.
