Pertinent Questions - 6

Our current pertinent question about tongues concerns the primary issue between continuationists (who believe all the gifts continue to the present day) and cessationists (who believe certain of the gifts have ceased to function) which is: Is the gift of tongues for the church today? The answer to this question needs development so we are taking a quick parenthetical break from tongues specifically to discuss spiritual gifts in general. Last time I introduced a common notion among cessationists that the spiritual gifts as listed in the New Testament may be categorized as either sign gifts, temporary gifts or as permanent gifts. We covered the permanent gifts and noted that:

  1. These gifts do continue to the present;
  2. They are very practical and useful in church life and;
  3. They are much less eye-catching and spectacular than most of the other gifts.

Moving on in reverse order, then, temporary gifts are those gifts which were also necessary to establishment of the early church, yet at some point were no longer needed or were not intended to extend indefinitely in the church. These gifts, more than the permanent gifts, also involved an overt, direct manifestation of God's power but in ways maybe not so obvious as to be signs to unbelieving witnesses. In this category I would put: prophecy; word of wisdom; word of knowledge; discerning of spirits; apostleship and maybe the interpretation of tongues. Why these? We will talk more about apostleship later and why it is important to the arguments of the cessationist so let's focus for now on the speaking gifts I noted.

Let me digress for a moment to note that the two 'words of wisdom...knowledge' are not easily categorized. It could be that the Holy Spirit grants a special measure of insight or logic to a believing individual which he utilizes freely on occasion on behalf of the church at critical times of making hard choices. If that is the case I would probably be more inclined to include them in the permanent category. But because Paul uses the phrase 'word of wisdom/knowledge' most scholars agree the gifts involved supernatural revelation from the Lord. The difference between the two probably involved the nature of the revelation. Was it insight and general philosophy for life or was it a specific bit of information needed for a particular situation? Again these two are difficult to classify precisely. But if they were direct revelation then I would say they fell into a subset of prophetical utterance.

At this point I think it is important for us to be exacting in our definition of prophecy. Why? Because there is a past and current trend of dumbing down the idea of New Testament prophecy to mean something less than a direct and specific revelation from God. Instead, they say, New Testament prophecy was (and is) more of an impression, a feeling, or a prompting, if you will, than a direct message from God which the prophet is intended to repeat and declare to his listeners. In his 2009 paper, Is the Gift of Tongues for Today?(p. 12), Robert Picirilli quotes two very well known and well respected evangelical theologians with regard to this matter. From D. A. Carson's Showing the Spirit (p. 169) he shares:

What preacher has not had the experience, after detailed preparation for public ministry, of being interrupted in the full flow of his delivery with a new thought, fresh and powerful, interrupting him and insinuating itself upon his mind, until he makes room for it and incorporates it into his message -- only to find after the service that the insertion was the very bit that seemed to touch the most people, and meet their needs? Most charismatics would label the same experience a "prophecy."

And from a 2005 interview by Tim Challies with Wayne Grudem, Grudem says:

In people's actual prayer lives as well as in the personal conversation of the pastor in the pulpit to the congregation, people talk about the Lord leading them and guiding them in specific ways. Sometimes in ways it sounds very much like the gift of prophecy to me, but they don't call it prophecy. They call it prompting or leading. I am thankful for all of that and I am very comfortable being in a home fellowship group where people pray and are willing to say how they think the Lord is leading them and guiding them as they pray and what He brings to their minds. And they don't call it prophecy. But I'm thinking, "That sure looks like prophecy to me.",

There are several problems with this approach to New Testament prophecy, and Picirilli addresses them in full, but for the sake of space let me quickly point out two critical issues. One is that those who take this low view of New Testament prophecy are not all that concerned about accuracy. In other words it is quite possible the prophet can be mistaken. In his book Charismatic Chaos (pp. 78-81), John MacArthur cites and discusses the modern 'Kansas City Prophets' who claim to receive revelation from the Lord but are satisfied if 'two-thirds' of them are on the mark. Hardly reassuring for the listener deciding whether to comply with the new revelation! The second problem is that while I strongly affirm that the Holy Spirit does prompt and lead and guide believers with inner urgings and unbidden thoughts, there is no need to make this equivalent with true revelatory prophecy. In fact, as Picirilli suggests, I think this should be the experience of every believer. But Paul makes it clear that the gift of prophecy is not given to every believer (nor is any other gift). That creates a bit of a disconnect in this approach to prophecy.

So if we define prophecy as God speaking directly to someone for the purpose of that person then sharing the revelation, then we easily see that this gift was needed by all the various churches during that period of time in the first century before the canon of the Scriptures was completed. But afterward? Prophecy was not needed anymore since the church now had God's full revelation. Since discerning of spirits was used to verify individual claims of prophecy it, too, was no longer needed.
