Definitions

What we are seeing today is a further development of charismatic teaching and practice. For brevity's sake we can trace the third wave of Pentecostalism (or charismatic teaching) to the late 1970s/early 1980s and especially to John Wimber and what eventually became known as the Vineyard Church movement. Whereas the main difference between the 'first' and 'second' wave had to do more with the ones involved (i.e., members of a traditional Pentecostal denomination in the first wave versus the second wave intrusion into traditionally non-Pentecostal groups) the third wave is different by way of its actual teachings about charismatic tongues.

What do I mean by that? Picirilli, in a paper presented at the 2009 FWB national convention, notes that "These phenomena [tongues and other sign gifts], however (and here the Vineyard churches break with traditional Pentecostalism), are not evidences of baptism in the Spirit and are not to be expected for every believer" (p. 2). He then summarizes the views of what he calls a 'mildly charismatic' element of Christendom represented by Christian thinkers like Wayne Grudem and D.A. Carson who are outside the traditional circles of the charismatics. Picirilli believes that, though there are a few distinctions, the main difference between these 'mildly charismatic' and the true 'third wave' is more a matter of "emphasis" than of doctrinal differences on the subject. The highlights of their positions are:

  • Both tongues and prophecy in 1 Corinthians and the church today, are in important respects different from their earlier appearances in the Bible. New Testament prophecy is not like Old Testament prophecy. The tongues at Corinth were different from those in Acts 2.
  • Both gifts are therefore less spectacular than usually thought. Prophecy is “speaking merely human words to report something God brings to mind.” Tongues are not human languages but a language for prayer, preferably exercised in private.
  • Neither gift is required for all Christians, although they are valuable and might be experienced by any believer. Such “gifts of the Spirit” are as much events as endowments to be possessed by persons.
  • Neither gift signifies advanced spirituality, but prophecy in the public assembly is a sign of God’s blessing and tongues enhance one’s prayer life. (Is the Gift of Tongues for Today?, p.3)

Picirilli expresses a sense of 'gratification' (p. 3) in that the arguments of those who resisted the 'second wave' onslaught of Pentecostalism have apparently proven effective. He notes that the 'third wave' and the 'mildly charismatic' agree that:

  1. The tongues in Acts 2 were human languages and that today's charismatics are not speaking human languages.
  2. That speaking in tongues is not the evidence of the baptism of the Holy Spirit and that Acts 2 does not record a kind of "Spirit baptism" that every Christian needs to experience subsequent to salvation.
  3. That tongues are not for every believer and need not be used in public at all. (Ibid.)

That brings us up to date for the most part with the history and development of the Pentecostal or charismatic gift of tongues. Next, we will start on the Biblical passages and the issues at hand.
