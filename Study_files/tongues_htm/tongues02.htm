Brief History

The controversy in the Christian community over charismatic tongues and other phenomena dates all the way back to the second century. Ronald Baxter in his book Charismatic Gift of Tongues (pp. 111-13) relates that in AD 150 Montanus claimed to be a prophet and to possess such gifts. According to Earle Cairns in his Christianity Through the Centuries (p. 102), Montanus was originally concerned with formalism in the church and the rise of the status of the clergy but his reaction developed into such an extreme position that it was eventually condemned as heresy and was labeled Montanism. Philip Schaff in History of the Christian Church (vol. 2, p. 417) expresses doubt that it should be seen as heresy but says rather it was ‘overstraining’ and ‘excessive’ but for the most part orthodox in doctrine. Nevertheless, Montanus, along with two women named Maximilla and Priscilla (who, according to Baxter, had both abandoned their husbands to follow Montanus) began to teach in the region of Phrygia. They gained great notoriety and influence (several sources say the women especially were exalted by their followers almost even to a place of preeminence over Jesus) to the point that Tertullian, an early church father, bishop of Carthage and acclaimed as a great apologist, eventually joined them around AD 200. The main point of contention between Montanists and the mainstream church was whether the same sort of inspiration which produced the New Testament writings continued or not. Montanus said it did and further claimed that he himself possessed it (as noted he was eventually labeled a heretic as a result). The other manifestations, such as speaking in tongues, played a secondary role but nonetheless contributed to the strife.

The point is to show all this is not new, though it was somewhat rare. According to John F. MacArthur, Jr., (Charismatic Chaos, pp. 285-86) there was no other significant outbreak of tongues until the seventeenth century: among some French protestants sometimes called the Cevennol prophets; and some Roman Catholics known as the Jansenists; and, in America, Mother Ann Lee's group, known as the Shakers. One other group appeared in the early nineteenth century, sometimes referred to as Irvingites after Scottish Presbyterian pastor Edward Irving. All these groups, as the Montanists, are usually characterized as unorthodox, fanatical and/or heretical.

But the current confusion and disagreement has its roots in the early twentieth century. In 1900 Charles Parham taught and led Christian meetings and schools in Topeka and in Kansas City in which members began to speak in tongues. In 1906 on Azusa Street in Los Angeles was a mission in which the same phenomenon broke out. It was from these events that many Pentecostal groups we see today first emerged and later developed (e.g. the Foursquare Gospel churches, Assemblies of God, various Pentecostal churches and Churches of God). For the first fifty years or so, the movement, and its belief that the required baptism of the Holy Spirit is necessarily evidenced by speaking in tongues stayed, as Robert Picirilli (What the Bible Says About Tongues, p. 2) puts it, ‘more or less within the confines of [those] particular denominations.’ This is often referred to as the first wave of Pentecostalism.

We will next take a look at the second and third waves of the Pentecostal movement which has had a much wider impact on the rest of Christianity.
