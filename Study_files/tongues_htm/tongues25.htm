Pertinent Questions - 16

In the previous segment we were continuing our comparison between New Testament speaking in tongues and what is happening today. We saw then that the general atmosphere in many (most?) of the churches practicing tongues seems to be disorderly and chaotic according to the testimony of those who have attended such. That sort of atmosphere is in direct contradiction to Paul's instructions in I Corinthians 14. We also debunked the idea that those of us who do not practice 'tongues' have somehow quenched the Holy Spirit's work in us and His will for us because we have not actively sought that gift. Scriptures do not command us to seek it nor do we see examples of anyone seeking it, even those to whom it was clearly given. Further I have spoken to those who felt like they needed to seek it and did so by praying and asking God to bring it to them. But He did not in some cases. How does this square with the idea of some charismatics that it is God's will for all believers to experience the Spirit's fullness through tongues? It does not.

Another corollary to this is how Acts 2:2-4 describes the way that first group on the day of Pentecost seemed to be overwhelmed by the Spirit. Thus what is implied sometimes from this is that the gift of tongues and some of the other charismatic goings on (like holy laughter, rolling on the floor, falling down and becoming stiff as a board, etc. which we have not addressed because there is no biblical basis for them whatsoever) are things completely controlled by the Holy Spirit and when He decides to move that way, a person is overwhelmed and controlled by that manifestation of power in that moment. The person cannot help but do it.

But is that true? I would not presume to dogmatically state what the Day of Pentecost looked like nor what sort of control those affected had or did not have. It would seem to have been a profoundly powerful experience. Could they have resisted the impulse to speak in tongues at that moment? That is somewhat of a moot question because the event was of God and served His purposes, and they were genuine believers in accord with God and each other. In other words, they were willing to be used as instruments of His plans and so they had no reason nor any inclination to resist what God sent to them.

However, having said all that, I do believe that they did have to ability to not continue speaking. Or perhaps a better way to put it, is that when the time came to be quiet they did not continue to speak out in these foreign languages as if they could not control themselves. What is the proof of that? Well, in v. 14 Peter and the other Apostles stand and he (Peter) begins to preach to the crowd. He had to speak up because we see later in v. 41 that there were at least 3000 people listening. It is hard for me to imagine that as he preached the others were running around speaking out in tongues and creating a disturbance and being a distraction. I am inclined to think that when Peter started speaking the others got quiet so he could be heard by the crowd. Is it absolutely certain I am right about this? No, because I was not there. But it seems to be a very reasonable assumption.

More importantly it is also in accord with what Paul says to the Corinthians in chapter 14:

  • 27 If anyone speaks in a tongue, let there be two or at the most three, each in turn, and let one interpret.
  • 28 But if there is no interpreter, let him keep silent in church, and let him speak to himself and to God.
Paul fully expects that there will be times in the Corinthian church services where several will want to speak in tongues. But he puts limitations on the number of speakers. This meant that any others beyond two or three had to keep quiet. This obviously means that they could decide to not allow their mouths to fly open and emit foreign languages which they did not understand themselves. Further if no one with the gift of interpreting such foreign languages was present then none were allowed to exercise this gift. Again this strongly presumes that such gifts were under the control of the one having the gift.

And in point of fact Paul makes this exact statement about another speaking gift:

  • 29 Let two or three prophets speak, and let the others judge.
  • 30 But if anything is revealed to another who sits by, let the first keep silent.
  • 31 For you can all prophesy one by one, that all may learn and all may be encouraged.
  • 32 And the spirits of the prophets are subject to the prophets.
Though he greatly prefers prophecy to tongues, Paul puts similar restrictions on the number of prophets who should share in a given service. Any who were not given a turn in a given service were to keep quiet and wait until another time. This way things were not disorderly and it kept the services to a reasonable amount of time so they did not all become discouraged. This was possible because the prophets had control over the spirit of prophecy within them. In other words it does not seem that if they received a prophecy they were then therefore compelled to speak. They had the ability to remain silent.

Though Paul did not make this exact statement as he gave instructions about tongues in the preceding verses, it seems reasonable to apply this principle to the other speaking gifts, indeed, to all spiritual gifts. Anyone who has a particular spiritual gift usually derives satisfaction from exercising it, whatever it may be -- teaching, giving, evangelizing, helping, administration and so on. But they also should have or should develop discernment about what constitutes an appropriate time to employ their gift. For instance, I love to teach but if I try to teach someone who has no interest in my subject matter, I do little or no good and perhaps do harm. Just so, some people don't want or need to be evangelized. Some people don't want or need to be helped. You get the point. Paul is essentially telling the church in Corinth that the way they are doing worship is, in fact, counterproductive because it was out of control. He is urging them to wisely and maturely restrain themselves and regain control so all can be edified, not just the self-centered ones.

All this contradicts those modern charismatics who claim that speaking in tongues and those other "manifestations" of the Spirit cannot be helped or controlled.
