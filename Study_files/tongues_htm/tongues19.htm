Pertinent Questions - 10

We have begun looking at that category of spiritual gifts we label as the sign gifts. As we have seen, it is clear from Scripture (cf. Mark 16:17; I Corinthians 14:22) that the gift of speaking in tongues is part of this class. These gifts with their overt supernatural manifestations were intended to show forth God's power and help validate the new way to God through faith in Jesus Christ as the perfect sacrifice which atoned for our sin. But the question for us now is: "Are they still being given to believers by the Holy Spirit even today?" Continuationists say, "Yes, all the spiritual gifts listed in the Bible continue in the church today." Cessationists say, "No, some of them (the permanent gifts) continue, but the speaking gifts connected with prophetical utterance ceased when the New Testament was completed (aka temporary gifts) and, likewise, the sign gifts also ceased." Why do each of the groups take their positions? What are their reasons? In this segment we'll consider and critique the view of the continuationist.

The main argument of the continuationists seems to be that since these gifts were part of the early church as revealed in the New Testament then they likewise must be valid for the church today. In other words, continuationists insist that since I Corinthians 12-14 treats all the gifts as functioning and since this passage is given to the modern day church for guidance as much as it was given to the first century church, then consequently all the gifts must be functioning today else the passage would be useless. This is a simple but, nevertheless, somewhat compelling argument. Christians, all of us, do look back (and appropriately so) to the New Testament, for patterns, for precedents and for principles to help us formulate church life today. We concede that point.

However, it is incorrect to suggest that just because a passage deals with a particular issue which existed in the first century automatically means that the same issue or condition will exist in perpetuity. Is there a biblical example we can use to support that last statement? Robert Picirilli points out an example in the very same epistle, namely, I Corinthians 11:2-16 which deals extensively with a woman's head covering at church (Is the Gift of Tongues for Today?, p. 5). Obviously many in today's church (at least in Western culture) are no longer concerned with this particular custom. Are we to suggest then that either: 1) We are in error and women in church should immediately cover their heads if praying?; or 2) The passage is obsolete and of no use to us so we can safely ignore it? I think neither of these options is necessary nor correct. I do not think it is necessary for cultural changes to be altered as long as they are neutral in effect (all the ladies will likely say "Amen!"). And certainly it is not correct to declare any of the Bible obsolete.

To help illustrate consider another verse -- Romans 16:16. Must we begin to conform to this standard of greeting? I think not (this time all the men will say "Amen!"). Yet the principle of sincere, warm and affectionate greetings between believers is certainly not obsolete. We simply shake hands and hug for the most part. The point is that just because a passage addresses a practice that happened in the first century does not always mean that the same practice exists today. But neither is the passage less valuable to us because the principles it teaches can go beyond the specific circumstance addressed. To wit, the overarching principles of I Corinthians 12-14 especially concern the need for believers to have humility, love and a desire to edify each other when they employ their spiritual gifts. Those principles can be easily gathered and taught today even if the main gift causing concern to Paul at the time (namely, speaking in tongues) is no longer given by the Holy Spirit.

Another primary argument often made by continuationists is to demonstrate that I Corinthians 13:8-10 does not establish beyond a shadow of a doubt that tongues (and, by extension, all the sign gifts) would cease in this present era. At this point I would like to quote Picirilli extensively from his already referenced paper (p. 6):

This is no doubt an important part of their argument, for the simple reason that many cessationists have interpreted the words of verse 8 (“tongues … shall cease”) to mean cease early in the present age. Both Carson and Grudem proceed along the following lines. The cessation of tongues (v. 8) will occur when “that which is perfect” (v. 10) comes. At present the gifts of prophecy (directly stated) and tongues (clearly implied) represent what is partial (v. 9), to be done away with when perfection comes. This will take place at the coming of Christ (the parousia) at the end of this age.

I will not attempt further development of their view of this passage since I am in fundamental agreement with them. Carson may think -- I am not sure -- that all non-charismatics believe that these verses point to that cessation. If so, he is wrong. I have never used that passage in defense of the cessation of the sign gifts. Consequently, his exegetical conclusion, that the passage does not prove cessation, has no effect on my position. I insisted, in my commentary, that “the point about these three gifts of the Spirit is that they represent the imperfect and partial work of the Spirit in us in the present age. All such gifts are temporary, destined to be replaced by something far better." Grudem is aware that at least one cessationist agrees with him that 1 Corinthians 13:8-10 does not foretell the cessation of tongues and prophecy during the present age.

The point to be made, here, is simply this: just as this passage does not tell that any of the gifts were intended to cease during the present age, neither does it tell that they were meant to continue throughout the age. It is one thing to show that the verses do not prove cessation. It is quite another to show that they require continuation. Assuredly, the perfection of the age to come will replace all our present imperfections and partial experience of the things of God. At that point everything characteristic of our present incompleteness will be done away, including our imperfect worship, our preaching and teaching, our ministering or showing of mercy. Paul’s point is that all of the gifts will pass away then. That falls very short of demonstrating that some of the gifts, whose purpose was temporary, did not pass away even earlier. Indeed, Carson and Grudem think that apostleship has passed from the scene, so the passage allows, in their view, for the cessation of at least one of the gifts long before the second coming. And if there are no more apostles, then the apostolic era has passed and we are in a different era.

That last about the apostles we will explore further in the next segment.
