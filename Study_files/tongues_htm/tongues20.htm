Pertinent Questions - 11

In the prior segment we observed the differing opinions of continuationists and cessationists about whether speaking in tongues (and sign gifts, in general) continue today. We also examined the two main arguments of the continuationists. The first is that since epistles were written for the modern church as well as the early church and since these writings treated the sign gifts as active then it follows they would also be active today, or else the passages would be worthless to us. We saw a couple of examples of cultural things that have changed and are no longer in existence in the modern church yet the passages are still valuable to today's believers for the underlying principles they teach.

The second argument from continuationists revolves around their refutation of I Corinthians 13:8-10 as a passage which absolutely proves that sign gifts would cease early in the church age. The argument hinges on how one interprets the phrase "that which is perfect." Does it refer to the completed canon of Scripture or to the return of Christ? Certainly either is a plausible possibility. But since it is conceded that the passage cannot be used to decisively disprove the existence of tongues in today's church, it may seem that the continuationist has won the point. However, as we saw, neither does the passage positively proclaim that all the gifts would absolutely continue until the return of Christ. Paul is downplaying the relative importance of any and all the gifts of the Spirit (which will, at some point, cease) when compared to love, which is the first of the fruits of the Spirit (cf. Gal. 5:22) and will always continue. So while we concede that this passage from the 'love chapter' does not require a person to believe that the sign gifts ceased when the Bible was completed, we also note that neither does it require a person to accept that all the gifts must therefore necessarily continue afterward.

At this point, it is probably a good time to reiterate and acknowledge that there is no passage which clearly delineates the expiration date or which plainly declares the permanency of any spiritual gift. As I have stated earlier, that truth is what allows genuine and well-meaning believers in Jesus to honestly disagree about this sensitive issue. That is why we must consider those passages and principles which surround the issue and try to make sound evaluations and decisions accordingly. So it is not enough to simply attack the arguments of the continuationists and critique them as lacking in real substance. Cessationists must also try to build a case to support their view. Is there one to be made? I think so. It has to do with the one spiritual gift we have not yet really examined, that of apostleship.

The vast majority of evangelical Christians today would agree (I think) that the office of an apostle has passed from the scene. In other words, there is no one whom God has installed in such an position today. Why do we say that? Because we see implied in Acts 1:21-22 and clearly stated in I Corinthians 9:1 that for someone to be called as an apostle, he had to have been personally and directly associated with Jesus Himself. That, of course, is no longer possible since Jesus has been in heaven with the Father for some 2000 years. Some may argue that Jesus had already ascended when He called Saul to be the Apostle Paul but even Paul acknowledged that this was an exceptional situation (cf. I Corinthians 15:8) which seems unlikely to be repeated. And, in fact, even some in his day doubted his authority and he did, at times, have to validate his calling.

And that brings us to an important point. Since Paul was an exception, how was he confirmed as a genuine apostle? In II Corinthians 11-12 he writes an extended defense of his apostolic authority which concludes with 12:11-12:

  • 11 I have become a fool in boasting; you have compelled me. For I ought to have been commended by you; for in nothing was I behind the most eminent apostles, though I am nothing.
  • 12 Truly the signs of an apostle were accomplished among you with all perseverance, in signs and wonders and mighty deeds.
Note his reference to 'the signs of an apostle' which involved 'signs, wonders and mighty deeds'. This is in accordance with another passage which speaks to the exercise of the sign gifts -- cf. Hebrews 2:3-4. Though I will not directly quote him, I need to acknowledge once again my indebtedness to Robert Picirilli for most of what follows. The passage in Hebrews outlines the progression of the course of salvation. It began with the 'Lord' Himself speaking, and then continued to be preached and 'confirmed' by 'those who heard Him' to 'us.' Thus, three links or parts are described in the gospel chain. Jesus, of course, worked miracles to validate His own identity (cf. John 20:30-31). But we see that v. 4 refers to 'those who heard Him' (i.e., the apostles -- cf. I John 1:1) as likewise being validated by 'God also bearing witness both with signs and wonders, with various miracles, and gifts of the Holy Spirit,...' So there seems to be a close association between the sign gifts and the apostolic office.

This is confirmed with what actually happens in Acts and here I will quote Picirilli:

An attentive reading of Acts, especially the first several chapters, is interesting for its emphasis on the works of the apostles. The following are noteworthy:

  • 2:43: “Many wonders and signs were done by the apostles.” Chapter three provides a specific example.
  • 4:33: “With great power gave the apostles witness of the resurrection of the Lord Jesus.” Chapter five provides a specific example.
  • 5:12: “And by the hands of the apostles were many signs and wonder wrought among the people.” The following verses provide a specific example.
This has all the appearance of a deliberate pattern, one that links the signs and wonders with the ministry of the apostles. This does not mean, of course, that absolutely no one else performed such miraculous works. Both Stephen (Acts 6:8) and Philip (Acts 8:6, 13) were instruments of such power, but it seems likely that their gift came at the hands of the apostles (Acts 6:6). (Is the Gift of Tongues for Today?, p. 4.)

What does all this mean? We continue and (hopefully) conclude this part in the next segment.
