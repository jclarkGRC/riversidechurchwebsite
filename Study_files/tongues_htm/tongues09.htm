Biblical Passages - 5

In this segment we will conclude our overview of the biblical passages which record actual occurrences of someone exercising the gift of tongues or which address them in some other fashion. I Corinthians 14 falls into the latter category. Paul has set the stage for his commentary and instructions about the gift of tongues by reminding his readers that believers are to be unified as a body even though they are diverse as individuals. This unity is made possible for two reasons. One is that the one and only Holy Spirit is source of all the gifts and He decides who gets what. The second reason is that bearing spiritual fruit (especially that of Christian love) is more important than exercising spiritual gifts. All these principles are given by Paul in chapters twelve and thirteen which we examined in the last segment.

Having given some general principles Paul begins to specifically focus on the main issue with regard to spiritual gifts which seems to be causing most of the trouble in the church at Corinth. That issue was the inability of the Corinthians to properly evaluate the relative worth of the gifts in relationship to one another. Recall that in 12:31 Paul encourages them to 'desire the best gifts' before sharing with them the overriding principle of love in chapter thirteen. Now in chapter fourteen he returns to the idea of the Corinthians seeking out the more desirable gifts -- cf. v. 1.

To begin he compares and contrasts the gifts of prophecy and speaking in tongues. It is obvious from the outset that Paul prefers the gift of prophecy and values it more than the gift of tongues. This may seem a little unfair when we remember that Paul already said that the Holy Spirit determines who gets what. If that is so and we have no say in it and we are all part of the same body, then shouldn't all gifts be valued the same? However Paul's reason for emphasizing prophecy and devaluing tongues explains a lot -- cf. vv. 2-5. Paul's concern above all else is that the church be edified through the exercise of spiritual gifts. This desire is in accordance with chapter thirteen's emphasis on love because it seeks the best interest of others.

Paul elaborates on this theme a good bit in vv. 6-19. The key issue seems to be understanding. It was possible for edification to happen with tongues if an interpreter was present to explain what was being said (although how cumbersome that can be). But with prophecy which was spoken in an understandable language, there would always be understanding (without the interruptions). We'll come back to some of these verses as we look at some of the specific questions about tongues later.

The need for Paul's instruction about this is caused by the Corinthian's immaturity and lack of understanding -- cf. v. 20. They apparently had overvalued and glorified the gift of tongues. Why? Perhaps because it was more 'showy'? This idea fits within the scope of Paul's teaching and concern. That sort of selfish seeking for attention was exactly what he has been trying to correct -- cf. v. 4. Paul continues by revealing that the purpose of the gift of tongues was 'for a sign.' This, too, we will revisit and discuss at length. For now it is sufficient to understand that, again, while edification for the church was possible through tongues, this was not the main purpose of the gift.

The immaturity and ignorance of the Corinthians was causing trouble in the church. What sort of trouble was it causing? Apparently it was creating disorder and confusion in their worship services -- cf. vv. 33, 40. How so? Some scholars think that when the people of the church came together there was sort of a competition to see who would be able to speak or share their gift, maybe more to the point, to have the spotlight -- cf. 26-31. This 'spirit of rivalry' as Robert Picirilli calls it (I &II Corinthians, Randall House Commentary, p. 205) can be seen earlier in the book as factions debate about who they prefer as their leaders (cf. 1:11-13). Remember, the main purpose of Paul's letter was to address questions and confront problems of the church at Corinth.

Notice that there were others besides those with the gift of tongues which were contributing to the problem. The general lack of maturity was shared by others as well. So much more the need for them to hear and heed God's word -- cf. v. 39.

We will conclude this segment by noting two things. One is to recognize how little good Paul had to say about exercising the gift of tongues in public worship services. The second is how closely the disorderly worship services Paul is trying to correct in his day resembles what happens in so many worship services today which similarly emphasize speaking in tongues and other sign gifts.

Next we will begin looking at specific questions and try to find our answers in Scripture to help us in our understanding of this controversial issue.
