A second key element of the Wiccan Law, besides the Rede, which most Wiccans put much stock in is The Rule of Three (also known as the Law of Return or The Threefold Law or The Law of Attraction). Essentially, the thought is that whatever energy you send out, whether good or bad, positive or negative, will come back to you three times stronger. Scott Penczak compares it to a rubber ball off a wall which ricochets back at you harder than you sent it out (but never mind the laws of physics). (Sons of the Goddess, pp. 5-6). Though the multiplication aspect seems unique to the Craft, the concept of karma is common in many religions and philosophies (e.g., the old sayings "What goes around, comes around." and "Those who play with fire, eventually get burned.") because we commonly see it at work in life.

Note: Even Jesus made reference to this idea as a basic part of reality when, in response to Simon Peter’s attack on those arresting Him in the Garden of Gethsemane, in Matthew 26:52 He said " ... for all who take the sword will perish by the sword". Also Paul comments in Galatians 6:7 that " ... whatever a man sows, that he will also reap". Thus the basic idea has some validity even in Christian thought. Remember that most people will not accept a worldview that has no demonstrable truth whatsoever. So it is, that even in Wicca, false as it is, there are nuggets of verifiable or experiential truth to which its adherents cling.

For Wiccans, however, the Rule of Three has an implication which is very important for them. They insist that because they hold to this law it would make no sense for a genuine practitioner to ever cast a hex or a curse on someone because eventually it would come back to the spellcaster with much greater force. On the other hand if a witch seeks to use her magick for the benefit of others, that, too, will return three times stronger. Thus, there is much incentive to help and no reason to work evil. For many Wiccans this is a powerful argument for the basic goodness (or at least the benign neutrality) of the vast majority of Wiccans.

FYI -- Those witches who do make a practice of working magick toward negative, hurtful or evil ends would probably be characterized by most other Wiccans as either being involved in the religion for the wrong reasons or not far enough along in their journey (i.e., immature) or perhaps they are really of some other pagan belief and are being mistaken for Wiccan. Essentially they would be dismissed as being insincere in their practice of the Craft.

It is worth noting that, for Wiccans, this karma thing will be played out fully in this life. They would not subscribe to the notion of a judgment or consequences waiting for them after this life is over. Even in their version of reincarnation (which we will see a little later), each new life is a clean slate and a fresh start with no issues hanging over one’s head as he or she is introduced back into this world for continuing education.

FYI -- In fact some Wiccans (a minority) reject the Rule of Three precisely because of the overtones that there is some sort of a cosmic justice or morality. Such witches would likely maintain a firm belief that while a decent person should not seek another person’s harm there is no "punishment" waiting if they do decide to take a matter into their own hands. Such Wiccans would also likely not be big into a literal, personal God and Goddess but rather be more inclined to the cosmic forces philosophy. They would likely see reality in terms of "balance" rather than as a conflict between “good and evil.”

"We must honor The Code."

Wiccans place a great deal of emphasis on helping others (especially other Wiccans). Besides the obvious benefits with respect to the Rule of Three, there is another reason for this, which is the Wiccan Code of Chivalry (also called the Witch’s Code of Chivalry). There is very long version which I will not share here but a shorter version or a summary is:

Thou shalt have good character.
Thou shalt treat everyone with respect and dignity.
Thou shalt always read extensively.
Thou shalt always champion the Right & Good against injustice and evil.
Thou shalt actively involve in the society.
Thou shalt act with integrity, truth and justice.
Above all, Thou shalt live happily.
In his book, Wiccan Beliefs and Practices, Gary Cantrell comments on the Code:
The Wiccan Code of Chivalry is a concept that is not specifically spelled out in the Wiccan Law, but is one that many of us have adopted as part of our way of life. It is sometimes referred to as the Old Code, and is essentially derived from the medieval knight’s code of conduct. That code, as we envision it today. exemplifies a deep love of the Wiccan religion and of those who practice that religion. It is carried forth in some of the wording of an initiation wherein the initiate swears to defend the Lord and Lady and all those who love Them, in this life and all those sure to follow. Implicit in the Code is also the promise to protect and assist those who may not be able to defend or care for themselves. This implication extends beyond the limits or confines of our Craft world. There may be those who need or seek our help who are not Pagan, but they are every bit as deserving of receiving it as any member in our Coven. We must never get so caught up in our own spiritual realm that we ignore others around us. (p. 48.)

Again we see goals and attitudes which are worthy to be adopted. On one website where the entire code was displayed, in the comment area was a response by a non-Wiccan (presumably) who indicated that everyone should live by values like these. In his book Sons of the Goddess, Scott Penczak does not list the Code of Chivalry as such but in chapter five Living in the World: Roles and Responsibility has a section entitled Code of Honor under which he mentions the attributes of honesty, health, courage, compassion, knowledge and respect and how witches should strive to embody these things. (pp. 80-82.) He contends that witchcraft originally grew from the desire to help and heal others and so compassion should arise naturally from those who become part of the Craft.

Personal power

One reason Wiccans believe they should adhere to certain proper codes of honorable conduct is that they believe they have access to power about which most people have no clue. They would agree with the old saying that "With great power comes great responsibility". and they believe they can have this ‘great power.’ This access to power is one of the big attractions to Wicca we referred to in the Introduction. It is universal human experience to struggle with problems and want solutions. It is just as common for someone to desire a thing and seek a way to get it. In Wicca the answer to both these issues is the application of magick.

The targets of Wiccan spells can be anything and everything. A coven can decide as a group to work spells to aid in achieving world peace or to thwart a proposed construction project or other such "bigger" issues both globally or local. Or a witch can have a personal goal or problem and apply his or her knowledge and skills toward gaining the desire or solving the difficulty. In chapter seven entitled Spellcraft: The Tools of Life in his book Sons of the Goddess, Christopher Penczak gives instructions for making your own spells and then gives examples and hints for a wide variety of applications. Among these are: protection spells for home, traveling, and binding someone who means to hurt you; mind spells for improving concentration, relieving stress, and improving eloquence; love spells for improving your personal attractiveness, improving your social life, and meeting that special someone; warrior spells for aiding you in competitive activities; money spells for gaining employment and for quick cash; and more. (pp. 135-158.)

All the spells Penczak shares involve many different aspects including which phase of the moon is best for the type of spell it is, which day of the week is best, what colors are associated, and so on. Steve Russo lists some of the "tools" that Wiccans use in their spell craft among which are crystals, stones, herbs, candles, colors, cords, words, chants, poetry, other hand-made objects and personal items. (What’s the Deal with Wicca?, pp. 50-54.) Also potions and poppets and symbols of power and other aids to spellcraft can be created out of these and other materials.

To show how this works generally, each of these different items has values or qualities assigned to it. For instance, according to some charts, the mineral fluorite is associated with mental acuity while cinnamon can be burned to stimulate intelligence and the color yellow is also associated with intelligence.

Note: Usually there is general consensus on the more common and popular materials (stones, herbs, colors, etc.) and their primary attributes and what they should be used for in spell casting, potions, etc.. But there can also be widely divergent opinions and information according to various books and authors. For instance almost all references and charts will assign to ‘white’ the quality of ‘purity’ but for ‘green’ one may say ‘nature’ while another says ‘prosperity’ while yet another says ‘growth.’ Needless to say, this is not an exact science.

Thus a spell to improve one’s mental capacities might include having the fluorite on hand and using a yellow candle to light a pile of cinnamon while intoning a chant or rhyme to embue the mineral with energy which you then could carry around with you to perpetually sharpen your wits. This is a very simple example (and very incorrect, I’m sure) but nevertheless that’s the basic idea.

So, as you might imagine, knowledge is the key to working magick. That explains why "how-to", "step-by-step" recipes for spells, as well as glossaries of terms, and tables of minerals and herbs and their attributes, and charts of lunar phases, and so forth, are more common than not in Wiccan books. People want to find out how to work spells to get the things they need and want. That’s also why reading and learning and recording one’s experiences is emphasized. It all goes back to finding out the most effective way to raise, channel and direct the energy needed to accomplish the witch’s purpose. The more knowledge and experience a witch gains, the more powerful he or she becomes. Wiccan author Raven Grimassi shares what he calls the "Eight-Fold Path" which a practitioner must master in order to become

an Adept or Master/Magus of the Arts:
  1. Mental discipline through fasting and physical disciplines.
  2. Development of the Will through mental imagery, visualization, and meditation.
  3. Proper controlled use of drugs (hemp, peyote, mescaline, alcohol).
  4. Personal power, thought projection, raising and drawing power.
  5. The Keys: ritual knowledge and practice. Use of enchantments, spells, symbols, and charms.
  6. Psychic development and dream control.
  7. Rising upon the Planes. Astral projection and mental projection.
  8. Sex Magic, sensuality, and eroticism. ...
The Eight-Fold path is a guideline to those who are interested in what the ancients believed to be the necessary steps to personal power. A personal study of each of these aspects will provide the magician with the tools through which deeper levels of magickal knowledge can be obtained. (Wiccan Magick: Inner Teachings of the Craft, pp. 186, 189.)

In essence, Wicca teaches that each person has the power to create his own reality through witchcraft. Steve Russo cites Gavin and Yvonne Frost who write:

No one, least of all kindly Jesus or gentle Buddha, expects or wants you to live in abject misery. So what is wrong with your life that needs fixing today? Name it; then fix it. Consider what spell will be useful, get the equipment you will need, and get out of that chair and do it. We reminded you earlier that the gods help those who help themselves. You must put something into motion. It only takes a small but simple push; but once it is in motion, the result will be unbelievable. Use the following affirmation: Witchcraft power, give me a new life. (The Magic Power of White Witchcraft, p. 230 as cited in What’s the Deal with Wicca?, p. 61)

Happy holy-days to you

A large part of Wiccan experience is the observation of days of power: esbats and sabbats. An esbat, sometimes known as the Full Moon Ritual is observed on ... you guessed it ... nights when there is a full moon. A full moon happens every 28½ days so there are at least thirteen esbats in a given year. This is seen as an especially powerful time for magick because of the power of the moon, which is associated with the Goddess, is at its peak. It is seen as a time for work since the opportunity for so much more energy is available.

FYI -- Steve Russo comments that Wiccans see the moon as a mystic source of energy because it reflects light (i.e., power) from a hidden sun and because the moon has a strong effect on such things as tides and the cycles of women and animals (What’s the Deal with Wicca?, p. 55). It is a widely shared belief among police officers and ER workers that nights with full moons will usually be busy because people tend to behave more erratically on such occasions. This is one of those interesting, validating factoids that give Wiccans confidence in their worldview.

It is during the time of the full moon that Wiccans will perform an important ritual many call "Drawing Down the Moon" which is intended to charge the participant with divine energy. Miria Liguana and Nina Metzner share a simple ceremony for one or more to accomplish this which I summarize.

First, prepare/purify yourself with a self-blessing and/or a ritual bath. Find a quiet place where the full moon can be seen clearly and easily. Cast a sacred circle in which to perform the ritual. Read/recite out loud a version of The Charge of the Goddess and focus on its meaning for the purpose of grounding and centering yourself. Then take out your special ‘Moon mirror’ (a mirror blessed, charged and used only for Wicca rituals) and hold it so the moon is reflected in it. Speak the following chant,
“You are the Mother of the moon.
You are the Goddess strong and bright.
I am the sister (or brother) of the moon.
Let me be filled with Goddess light.”
and let yourself be filled with the Lady’s energy. The ritual is over when you can feel power build within and you feel ‘touched by the Lady'. Then it is a good time to cast other spells as you wish and conclude by discharging the energy and removing the circle, etc. as usual. (The Complete Idiot’s Guide to Wicca Craft, pp. 60-61.)
FYI -- The Charge of the Goddess is a well loved poem written from the perspective of the Goddess in which she instructs and makes promises to those who follow her ways. Because of what it contains and because it is used so frequently it is also an important anthem for Wiccans. Ashleen O’Gaea calls it "... the Charge that guides our understanding of the world and our interpretation of what happens in it". (The Family Wicca Book, p. 185.) There are several versions both older and modern, here is a popular shorter one written in 1897 by Charles G. Leland copied from www.fortunecity.com on a page entitled The Sacred Moon Circle:
Whenever you have need of anything,
one in the month and be it better when the moon is full,
then shall you assemble in some secret place and adore
the spirit of me, who am the Queen of all witches.
When I have departed from this world,
Whenever you have need of anything,
Once a month, and when the moon is full,
Ye shall assemble in some desert place
or in a forest all together join
To adore the potent spirit of your Queen
My mother, great Diana, She who fain
Would learn all sorcery yet has not won
Its deepest secrets, then my mother will
Teach her, in truth all things as yet unknown.
And ye shall all be freed from slavery,
And so ye shall be free in everything,
And as a sign that ye are truly free,
Ye shall be naked in your rites, both men
And women also: this shall last until
The last of your oppressors are dead.

The esbat, then, is the likely source of the classic stereotype of a coven gathering together in a secluded place with the full moon looming, for the purpose of brewing potions, casting spells and wildly celebrating in their worship of pagan, nature gods. While the accuracy of the stereotype would likely depend on the character of the coven, there is some kernel of truth to it. But since we are speaking of stereotypes this is as good a place as any to point out a false one, namely, the belief that Wiccans worship Satan. They do not. In fact they do not even believe he exists since they think he is a construct of early Christian authorities to frighten the general populace in order to keep them in line. Still many people outside Wiccan circles often confuse Satanism and witchcraft as being the same. Since witches do not want to have much, if anything, to do with Christian doctrine and they certainly do not conceive of themselves as evil, this can be a sore point for them.

FYI -- Of course Christians realize that whether or not they actually directly believe in or worship Satan himself is beside the point with regard to their eternal destiny. Any religious practice or belief which distracts someone from the one true God as revealed in the Bible and salvation through Jesus Christ is equally useful for the devil. One does not have to believe in Satan to serve his purpose, which is to destroy as many of God’s highest creation, mankind, as possible by separating them from the Lord.

But Wiccans also work magick during the other parts of the lunar cycle as well and the position of the moon affects the kind of spells they cast. Christopher Penczak advises that while many factors can come into play when trying to decide the best time to work a certain magick, one extremely important rule is "pay attention to the Moon!" He asserts that as the moon affects tides coming in and going out, so magickal energy is pushed in and pulled out by the moon’s phases. From new moon to full moon the power is rising until it reaches its peak and then it begins fading as it wanes back from full to new. (Sons of the Goddess, p. 134.)

Liguana and Metzner concur and offer advice about what sort of magick is best done under the various phases of the moon. According to them (The Complete Idiot’s Guide to Wicca Craft, p. 52.):

  • new moons (which cannot be seen) are especially appropriate for magick pertaining to beginnings or fresh starts (e.g., beginning a new job or career, trying to have a baby, starting a relationship, taking on a project or any new endeavor);
  • waxing moons (look like a backward ‘C’) are favorable for magick pertaining to growth and positive changes so casting spells to attract good things to you (e.g., love, wealth, luck, knowledge) are powerful at this time;
  • full moons are times when things come to fruition or fulfillment thus this is when your heart’s desire can be realized. The authors recommend that during the three days before, during, and the three days after the full moon, a witch should focus on casting spells invoking power, strength, healing and nurturing;
  • waning moons (look like a ‘C’) are times of ending or releasing things so banishing or completing magick is most powerful at this time (e.g., ending a bad relationship, losing weight, getting out of an encumbering responsibility).

Besides the esbats, monthly observances especially related to the phases of the moon, Wiccans also celebrate eight solar holidays generally referred to as sabbats. Just as esbats (and the moon) are associated with the Goddess, so are sabbats identified with the God and, in fact, tell a bit of his story. There are two types: the greater sabbats, sometimes referred to as being at the "cross quarters" of the year, which are based on old Celtic “fire festivals” which celebrated the cycles of agriculture; and the lesser which are celebrations based on the seasonal solstices and equinoxes, or the journey of the sun in a given year. Each of the sabbats has symbolic meaning to witches, that is, there is a reason (or reasons) for their celebration at each point of the wheel. The following information about each particular sabbat is a consensus from several sources:

  • Yule, being winter solstice, takes place on the day with the least amount of sunlight. However, from then on, daily sunlight begins to slowly increase. This is celebrated as the birth (or rebirth) of the God through the work of the Goddess. Thus life emerges and perseveres in the midst of darkness and death. This explains the popular use of holly, mistletoe, and evergreen at this season.
  • Imbolc is the first of three sabbats of increasingly growing light. The Goddess has recovered from giving birth to the God, regains her youth and becomes a maiden again. The God is still a child. It is a celebration of early stirrings and the renewal of life and fertility.
  • Ostara is the vernal equinox and now sunlight and darkness are equal in duration with the light beginning to prevail. Spring and fertility are here and the Lord, now a youth, and the Lady, still a maiden, come together as lovers. The earth is becoming green and it is at this time that the God is seen as the Green Man or Jack in the Green. Eggs, symbolic of life, are decorated and incorporated into the celebration.
  • Beltane is the second Celtic fire festival and third spring festival. The Lord is now mature and is the Lady’s partner and champion. She assumes her aspect as a young mother. The earth’s fertility and the abundance of life is in full evidence as summer’s almost here. Bonfires and Maypole dances are common during the observance of this sabbat.
  • Litha (also known as Midsummer) is the summer solstice. Sunlight now dominates the day and the God is at the peak of his power while the Goddess is now the mother of many children. However, it is also the turning of the tide and now darkness will progressively gain strength as the year ages.
  • Lammas is the third of the "cross quarter" sabbats and the first of three harvest festivals. The Lord is growing older and is losing strength while the Lady is also aging and moves from mother to crone. For now there is still plenty of light and the harvest is abundant but people’s minds begin to turn to thoughts of preparing for winter.
  • Mabon is the autumnal equinox and the second harvest festival. It equates to a Wiccan version of Thanksgiving. Light is now fading and the God is preparing to die. The Goddess is mourning his passing and her fall colors denote the withdrawal of her power from the land.
  • Samhain is the most important of the eight sabbats for Wiccans. It is the last harvest festival and is both the beginning and the end of the Wheel of the Year. The Lord is now in the underworld and the earth is ruled by the Horned God of the hunt until his rebirth at Yule. The Goddess is fully in her crone aspect and the land seems barren. Death is a central aspect of this sabbat and Wiccans take time to reflect on the passing of those they have known. Of course this date is also the more widely celebrated Halloween similarly associated with dark supernatural forces and beings.

How a witch celebrates a sabbat is up to her. Some may gather with members of their covens for a somber ceremony while others may plan a raucous party or there may be a little of both. Some solitary witches just do their own thing quietly and without much fanfare. There will almost always be some sort of ritual followed by the customary "cakes and wine (or ale)" observance. Steve Russo comments on how Wiccans see their holy days:

... Sabbats can be seen on three levels. First, they are times of religious worship for Wiccans to connect with the god and goddess. Second, Wiccans use these days of power for working their magick to help, heal, comfort, and protect their friends and loved ones. They receive help in the process from other deities. Third, these are times of celebration. Once the worship and work has been completed, it’s party time! (What’s the Deal with Wicca?, p. 56.)
