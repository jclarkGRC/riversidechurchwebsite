History:

We have already mentioned that there is no consensus regarding the true history of Wicca. But that doesn’t mean it’s a total mystery. A.J. Drew, in his book Wicca for Couples, asserts that

all Wiccans seem to agree ... that our religion is either based on ancient Pagan fertility religions or is itself a descendant of those same religions. (p. 32.)
Thus what we are dealing with is either:
  1. old ideas mixed with some contemporary thinking and a bit of modern marketing and, voila!, a new religion; or
  2. some of these old religions actually survived over the centuries being passed down from generation to generation until the time was right for it to re-emerge.
Which is right? Steve Russo poses and answers a question:
Does it sound confusing that Wicca can be both contemporary and centuries old? Here’s a brief explanation: Wicca’s core beliefs are the same today as they were centuries ago, only now they’ve been repackaged for the twenty-first century with a contemporary look and feel. (What’s the Deal with Wicca?, pp.18-19.)
Is he correct? Let’s see.

Ancient Roots

There is certainly some truth in the assumption that the roots of Wicca are pagan. Both the Bible and secular history record ancient and primitive cultures whose religious activity centered around the worship of fertility gods and goddesses whether Baal and Asherah of the Canaanites, or Woden and Freya of the Norse and Germanic cultures, or Pan and Diana of the Greeks or countless others. These ideas and practices have been around for millennia.

For some (e.g., Margaret Murray and Gerald Gardner -- more on them later) modern Wicca is a direct descendant of these ancient religions. We do not have space for a thorough examination so a general summary must suffice. According to those who hold to this view of Wiccan origins, the ancient cultures understood that there was a supreme deity but could not grasp enough to fully comprehend how to worship Him/Her/It. Instead they worshipped the localized sub-gods (such as those already referenced). Typically there was a god of the hunt and a goddess of fertility. This continued to evolve and develop over time. Since modern Wicca is largely from the United Kingdom there is a special interest in referencing the Celts and druids and their beliefs and practices (secretive, nature loving, moon and sun oriented, etc.) as primary ancestors of the Craft.

FYI -- Wikipedia cites a Professor Ronald Hutton as declaring that Wicca is "the only religion England has ever given the world:, (Wow ... thanks a lot, old chaps!)

Of course, when we speak of such things as this we’re talking about developments taking place over two or three thousand years ago while modern Wicca did not really re-emerge until the 1950’s. Why did it take so long and what happened to the followers of the "Old religion" during those years?

Again according to some Wiccans, several events caused this ancient and mysterious religion to have to go underground. The three waves of invasions by first the Romans, later the Saxons and finally the Normans into the English isles caused folks like the druids, pagans and witches to hide their beliefs or else be persecuted (History of Wicca}. That last invasion by the Normans took place late in the eleventh century around 1066 AD.

But the most damaging attacks came from Christianity. As Christianity became dominant in the Roman empire and later as the office of the Pope became more influential as the empire broke into city states and nations, the hostility against paganism was strong. In 1401 the English parliament passed the first laws against witchcraft which were harsh and became even harsher as time went on (Russo, What’s the Deal with Wicca?, p. 27). In 1484 Pope Innocent VII also encouraged people to be on the lookout for witches and in response someone printed The Witches Hammer, a manual to help those hunting witches (Ibid). Wiccans refer to these years (ca. 1450-1790) as the "Burning Times" because that’s what happened to many who were convicted of being witches (History of Wicca). It is estimated by some that across Europe and in the American colonies between 40,000 and 100,000 people were executed for the crime of witchcraft during this period (History of Wicca). Those who subscribe to this view assert that it was during these years that the "Old religion" was almost destroyed, its followers nearly exterminated and much of the writings and lore lost.

It only barely survived by once again going deep underground as it did during the aforementioned invasions. Practitioners most often performed their rites and ceremonies alone or in very small groups. Most of the information and practice that remained was passed down in the family from one generation to another. Thus the forbearers of what we know today as Wicca persevered but their religion was not in any way influential or even known about until the last fifty years or so.

Modern Revival

Whether all the preceding information is accurate or not, the contemporary movement of Wicca is quite real and quite traceable in its development. By far the most often cited name with regard to Wicca’s current manifestation is that of Gerald Gardner. By all accounts, he was an English civil servant and amateur archaeologist/anthropologist who spent many years in the Far East and was fascinated with some of the mystery religions there. Some might say his interest came naturally for, according to William C. Viser, an ancestor, Grissell Gardner, was burned at the stake for practicing witchcraft in 1610 (The Darkness Among Us, p. 124). He studied and engaged in several Eastern mystical sects and by the time he returned to England was an "avid occultist" (History and Background of Wicca). In 1939, he joined a group known now as the New Forest coven, a group of witches practicing in Dorset, England. In 1952 the last of the anti-Witchcraft laws were repealed and this freed Gardner to begin promoting his faith.

FYI -- The last person convicted under the Witchcraft Act in England was Helen Duncan in 1944. She allegedly had powers of clairvoyancy (knowing what is not in normal range of sight or hearing) and authorities feared she might betray plans for the Normandy invasion to German agents. She spent nine months in prison. (Russo, p. 28.)

Gardner always claimed that the coven he joined had been in continual existence over the centuries and thus had descended from ancient times intact except for a lack of written material regarding their practices. His concern was that the movement was dying out and he wanted to help revive it. In this attempt Gardner helped establish other covens (one of note was the Bricket Wood coven) and wrote several books with Witchcraft Today in 1954 and The Meaning of Witchcraft in 1959 being perhaps the two most influential. He is also credited with creating the first Book of Shadows (a workbook describing rituals, spells, etc.) around 1953 though some suggest he plagiarized some or much of it from other sources. Over the course of the years he interacted with several other notable occultists/witches such as Aleister Crowley, Doreen Valiente and Raymond Buckland. Toward the end of his life he served as "resident witch" at the Museum of Magic and Witch-craft in Castletown on the Isle of Man (Viser, p. 124). He died in 1964.

Other notables who came forward in the 1950s claiming to be members of old line covens which were surviving remnants of the ancient faith include Sybil Leek, Charles Cardell, Raymond Howard, Rolla Nordic and Robert Cochrane (Wikipedia). One who was initially influential in presenting and defending the idea that Wicca is the direct descendant of an old pre-Christian pagan faith was Margaret Murray. She wrote The Witch Cult in Western Europe and The God of the Witches both of which dealt with that premise (History of Wicca). But these others had little or nothing to do with Gardner. It is important to note that many of these others (who claimed to be of the "Hereditary" or "Traditional" forms of witchcraft) were often at odds with what came to be known as the "Gardnerian" form.

Much of the dispute seems to stem from differences in opinion about who had the best or most valid pedigree. An example of this conflict can be had concerning the man thought to be most responsible for introducing Wicca into the United States. Raymond Buckland, along with his wife Rosemary, moved to Long Island, New York from England in the early 1960’s. In their travels back and forth from the USA to the UK, they corresponded with and were influenced by Gerald Gardner. Finally they were initiated into the "Gardnerian" form of the Craft. Upon returning to New York they founded the Long Island coven (wikipedia.org). Buckland was very outspoken and many others practicing Wicca found him to be arrogant. A.J. Drew comments,

Some publications of the late 1960s and early 1970s indicate that in the eyes of many Hereditary Witches already practicing in the United States, Buckland seemingly declared himself the Pope of all Witches. You can see how this would cause a divide between the invading Gardnerian Wicca and the Hereditary Witches already in the United States who had no connection to Mr. Gardner much less Mr. Buckland. (Wicca for Couples, p. 42.)
FYI -- In his book, Mr. Drew continues discussing this conflict and refers to it as the Witch War. Though it mostly consisted of public disdain and verbal attacks directed at one another, sometimes it escalated to the point of competing witches or covens working destructive spells and negative magic against each other. (Oooooh...scary.)

But regardless of one’s opinion of Buckland it cannot be denied that he was extremely able in his promotion of Wicca. He wrote several influential books, among them Witchcraft -- Ancient and Modern, Witchcraft From the Inside, Buckland’s Complete Book of Witchcraft and The Tree: The Complete Book of Saxon Witchcraft (Ankerberg and Burroughs, What’s the Big Deal About Other Religions, p. 111).

In these years Wiccan thought and practice went in many different directions. Others who founded or heavily influenced various Wiccan groups include Alexander Sanders (Alexandrian Wicca); Mary Nesnick (Algardian Wicca -- an attempt to meld the Gardnerian and Alexandrian forms); Zsuzsanna Budapest (Dianic Wicca -- heavily emphasizes feminism); a practitioner called ‘Starhawk’ tried to reconcile the Gardnerian and Dianic traditions in her book Spiral Dance; and Steward and Janet Farrar who eventually left the Alexandrian form and decided to unveil Wicca completely to the public by publishing works such as Eight Sabbats for Witches (1981) and The Witches’ Way (1984). To show just how fluid Wicca is in general we should note that Buckland, after spending so much effort establishing the Gardnerian form in the USA, decided to leave it in 1973 and create a new sort which he called Seax-Wicca, which almost exclusively emphasizes the Saxon traditions. There are many other notable names among Wiccans such as Paul Huson, Jessie Wicker Bell (aka ‘Lady Sheba,’ self-styled ‘Queen of American Witches’), Silver Ravenwolf and Isaac Bonewits.

True Origins?

One last major point needs to be made before we leave off exploring the history of Wicca. In more recent years many Wiccans have concluded that the idea that the religion they practice is directly descended from an ancient religion through a continuous line is just so much hogwash. Those who take this position are critical of the scholarship of people like Gerald Gardner and Margaret Murray. Wikipedia cites Wiccan Jenny Gibbon who writes:

We Neopagans now face a crisis. As new data appeared, historians altered their theories to account for it. We have not. Therefore an enormous gap has opened between the academic and the ‘average’ Pagan view of witchcraft. We continue to use outdated and poor writers, like Margaret Murray, Montague Summers, Gerald Gardner, and Jules Michelet. We avoid the somewhat dull academic texts that present solid research, preferring sensational writers who play to our emotions.
Likewise, the Ontario Consultants on Religious Tolerance website cites Joanna Hautin-Mayer who comments:
We know tragically little about the actual religious expressions of the ancient Celts. We have a few myths and legends, but very little archaeological evidence to support our theories. We have no written records of their actual forms of worship, and the accounts of their culture and beliefs written by their contemporaries are often highly biased and of questionable historical worth. (History of Wicca)
The website goes on to note that Ms. Hautin-Mayer is extremely critical of works which propose the theory of a continuous existence of this kind of pagan religion which eventually became modern Wicca.

Some of those who reject the ‘ancient origins’ theory also question the motives and integrity of Gardner. For instance, A.J. Drew, in discussing the origins of the Witch War, points out how Gardner conveniently neglected to disclose the whole truth regarding the repealing of the Witchcraft Act:

When Mr. Gardner told us that it was only the English repeal of anti-Witchcraft laws in 1952 that allowed him and others to come forward, he forgot to mention that those remaining laws dealt primarily with fraud and not with religion. Other than those laws designed to protect an innocent public from fraudulent fortune tellers, the anti-Witchcraft laws had been abolished since 1736. (Wicca for Couples, p. 44.)
FYI -- Indeed, Russo remarks that in 1736, with King George II on the throne, Parliament changed the tone of the Witchcraft Act so that instead of convicted perpetrators being guilty of a capital offense they were rather treated as con artists for which the penalty was a fine or imprisonment (What’s the Deal with Wicca?, p. 28). So Gardner was not in mortal danger because of his choice of faith as is implied in his and others’ writings.

Thus many Wiccans have been forced to the conclusion that Gardner and some of the others did not actually uncover an ancient religion that had been concealed for centuries because of persecution, but rather he and they created it. A.J. Drew continues:

The religion that would change my life was invented only a decade before my birth and was sterilized when I was too young to know what sterility was. Yes, I did say invented. Although Mr. Gardner filled our culture with the belief that Wicca had been hiding from religious persecution between the beginnings of the Burning Times and England’s repeal of the final Witchcraft laws in 1952, the truth had become clear to all but our most novice. (Wicca for Couples, p. 43.)

Most of my sources agree with A.J. Drew that the vast majority of Wiccans understand that the "Old Religion" they practice is actually a fairly new phenomenon. They also agree, as we earlier asserted, that it does contain many elements which have been in existence for millennia. But these elements have come from many diverse sources. Thus modern Wicca is an amalgamation of teachings and practices from such things as Celtic and druidic legends, Gnosticism and Freemasonry, the occult and spiritism (remember Gardner’s association with Aleister Crowley?), Eastern mysticism and religions (such as Hinduism), shamanism and Native American religions, New Age philosophies and even science fiction.

You might think that the knowledge of this discrepancy between what the founding leaders claimed and the actual truth might throw up a red flag to one considering the Craft as an option for his or her worldview and life. That the openly acknowledged deception and/or ignorance might impede one’s interest in Wicca. That assumption would be incorrect. A typical attitude is likely reflected in the words of Wiccan author Silver Ravenwolf:

Wicca, as you practice the religion today, is a new religion, barely fifty years old. The techniques you use at present are not entirely what your elders practiced even thirty years ago. Of course, threads of ‘what was’ weave through the tapestry of ‘what is now’ ... in no way can we replicate to perfection the precise circumstances of environment, society, culture, religion and magick a hundred years ago, or a thousand. Why would we want to? The idea is to go forward with the knowledge of the past, tempered by the tools of our own age. (cited in History of Wicca)

Thus Wicca does have ancient elements of various movements and ideas. These attributes are old because they are all typical of the common characteristic human kind has manifested since the beginning no matter what the label may be. That characteristic is, namely, our sinful rebellion reflected in the attempt to create our own god and make our own rules and do our own thing. So Wicca is old in that it is simply another version of belief in the original lie from Satan (i.e., that we can be like God) and it is new in that this particular religion has its own flavor and teachings. Let’s see what some of those ideas are and how they are implemented.
