Falling Into Wicca:

Now that we have looked at some of the philosophical and theological foundations of Wiccan thought we will next look at some of the rules and principles which they hold dear. In other words the things they believe and practice in everyday life.

Early learning

When a person decides to give Wicca a try (we will look a little later at the question of why they might make this choice when we attempt to better understand how best to reach out to witches with the Gospel) he or she will naturally try to learn more about it. There are many avenues to gaining such knowledge. Books (such as those referenced in this study) are easily found in the major bookstore chains as the growth of Wicca has created a huge demand. Websites are also a popular place to find out about the Craft with the most visited probably being wicca.com and witchvox.com. There is also formal training available online at WitchSchool.com. Or there may be a more personal connection, for instance, the person may know a witch with advanced knowledge who is willing to mentor him or her one-on-one. Or the person may find and join a coven in order to be trained by them. It is not hard to find out more about Wicca these days and, as we noted in the introduction, more and more are doing so.

FYI -- According to Burroughs and Alupoaicei, WitchSchool has 300 instructors, more than 130,000 registered students and every online tool used by the major universities. (Generation Hex, p. 13)

The first steps

Whatever the case may be, after doing enough research and making the decision to become a witch, one of the first things the person will probably do is be dedicated (or initiated) into the Craft. In some cases they are being initiated into a particular tradition (or branch) of Wicca. If this is so, it will probably be a group thing with certain prescribed procedures in the ritual marking them as Gardnerian or Alexandrian or Dianic and so on. Or it could be a coven of eclectic (independent) witches who will have made up their own ceremony. Or it could be that the person wishes to be a solitary practitioner and thus she is dedicating herself.

Miria Liguana and Nina Metzner share a simple form (their main goal seems to be to encourage beginners by making things very easy) of the ritual in their book The Complete Idiot’s Guide to Wicca Craft. First they encourage the reader to pick a day when she is in a good frame of mind and then choose a private place and a quiet moment so she will be undisturbed. They tell the reader to at least take off her shoes (if not comfortable working skyclad) and create a sacred space by casting a magick circle using her athame and by using candles, holy water or a few other options. After everything is set:

Spend a few moments touching base with your own feelings. Sprinkle your sacred space with holy water. Take several deep breaths. Listen to the sounds around you. Once you’re sure you are in touch with yourself and aware of your surroundings, kneel down on your left knee. Put your right hand under your right heel and your left hand on your head. Then, say out loud: ‘All that is between my two hands I dedicate to Lady and Lord and to my growth in the Craft.’ Presto! You are dedicated to the practice and launched on the Wiccan path. (p. 16.)
FYI -- They also give instructions for a more formal dedication ceremony to those who want more elaborate. This ceremony involves having an altar in the middle of the sacred space upon which will be something (statuette, picture, some appropriate object) to represent the Goddess and God respectively. There is also needed: a grain mixture or a rope to mark out the circle; candles of yellow, red, blue, green and white as well as another small candle; incense; two small bowls or cups; salt; a symbol of the person herself (e.g., her wand if she has one, or a piece of jewelry she will wear only while working magic, etc.); a robe or cloak to wear. They also suggest inviting friends since it is more formal. The specific instructions for this ceremony are too lengthy to include here. (pp. 17-23.)

A couple of other things that a beginning witch will be encouraged, and will likely want, to do very early in her Wiccan experience is to make a personal tool of some sort (for most it is a wand, though many prefer the athame), and also to begin her Book of Shadows. For most witches, these items, more than anything else, will function as a representation of themselves with regard to their Wiccan beliefs. As a witch creates and embellishes her wand and adds to her Book over the years, they will reflect her growth in the knowledge and intuitive understanding of the Craft.

Basic principles

Speaking of knowledge, some of the first lessons a new witch will learn about Wicca will concern the two or three general truths which almost all those involved in Wicca recognize. These are as close to universal rules for Wicca as it gets.

First, we’ll look at what some call the Wiccan Law. There are various versions (as one might expect by now) but here we will see two -- a long one and a short one. I saw these in several places but took these versions from www.theblessedbee.com (so I could cut and paste it).

The Wiccan Rede (long version)

  1. Bide the Wiccan Law ye must, in Perfect Love and Perfect Trust.
  2. Live and let live -- fairly take and fairly give.
  3. Cast the Circle thrice about to keep all evil spirits out.
  4. To bind the spell every time, let the spell be spake in rhyme.
  5. Soft of eye and light of touch -- speak little, listen much.
  6. Deosil go by waxing moon -- dance and sing the Witches Rune.
  7. Widdershins go when the moon doth wane and the werewolf howls by the dread wolfsbane
  8. When the Lady’s Moon is new, kiss the hand to her times two.
  9. When the Moon rides at her peak, then your heart’s desire seek.
  10. Heed the North Wind’s mighty gale -- lock the door and drop the sail.
  11. When the wind comes from the south, love will kiss thee on the mouth.
  12. When the wind blows from the east, expect the new and set the feast.
  13. When the West Wind blows o’er thee, departed spirits restless be.
  14. Nine Woods in the cauldron go--burn them quick and burn them slow.
  15. Elder be ye Lady’s tree -- harm it not or cursed ye’ll be.
  16. When the Wheel begins to turn, let the Beltane fires burn.
  17. When the Wheel has turned to Yule, light the Log and let Pan rule.
  18. Heed ye flower, bush and tree -- by the Lady, Blessed Be.
  19. Where the rippling waters go, cast a stone and truth ye’ll know.
  20. When ye have need, harken not to other’s greed.
  21. With the Fool no season spend, nor be counted as his friend.
  22. Merry Meet and Merry Part -- bright the cheeks and warm the heart.
  23. Mind the Threefold Law ye should -- three times bad and three times good.
  24. When misfortune is anow, wear the Blue Star on thy brow.
  25. True in love ever be, unless thy lover’s false to thee.
  26. Eight words the Wiccan Rede fufill; and ye Harm None, do what ye will.

The Wiccan Rede (short version)

Bide the Wiccan Laws ye must
In perfect love and perfect trust.
Eight words the Wiccan Rede fufill,
An ye harm none, do what ye will.
Lest in thy self-defense it be,
Ever mind the rule of three.
Follow this in mind and heart,
And Merry Meet and Merry Part!
** FYI -- You may have noticed that the website calls these two versions the Wiccan Rede rather than the Wiccan Law. Some do that while others reserve the term Rede for the advice or counsel of the 26th line of the long version and the fourth line of the short version.

All versions of the Wiccan Law contain two key elements which witches would probably consider to be two of the most important pieces of wisdom for the Craft. The first element we alluded to in the FYI, the Wiccan Rede which goes, "An ye harm none, do what ye will" which essentially means that as long as you do not hurt someone else, you are free to do as you please. There are really no hard and fast rules which, as we noted in the Introduction, is a major attraction of Wicca, especially to younger people. Burroughs and Alupoaicei refer to this as "the cafeteria appeal". Listen as they comment:

... Wicca is personal and adaptable. ... They can create their own brand of Wicca that works for them. One Wiccan writes, ‘Really, there’s no one true or right way to practice the craft. Wicca is what you make of it.’ I (Marla) interviewed a teen named Jessica -- a soft-spoken articulate girl who wears a pentacle and observes the craft. When I asked her why she chose to follow Wicca, she said, ‘Wicca gives me room to breathe. It’s malleable. When I practice it, I feel free. But Christianity seems so rigid. My parental units tell me, "If you don’t do things exactly the way the Bible says, you’re going to hell."’ She smile ruefully but her pain was evident. ‘I’m surrounded by spastic Christians,’ she said. (Generation Hex, p. 37.)
FYI -- Though it looks good at first glance there is an obvious problem with trying to make the Rede work in the real world. How can we know for certain that in the choices we make no one else is being harmed? Often we think that our actions are harmless or that if we make questionable choices we only hurt ourselves. The truth is that we often cannot see the ultimate effects of our choices on others and, as the saying goes, "no man is an island". Furthermore, in Wiccan teachings there are no ultimate standards for determining what is good and bad, right and wrong. What may seem like a benign action to one could be a cancer to another. Whose standard is followed? Steve Russo cites a high school girl named Kaytee who
is going to use her first spell to help her get the part of Peter Pan in the school play. ‘It’s like prayer,’ she says. ‘I will project that I will get this part of Peter Pan in the musical, harm me none and for the good of all.’ (What’s the Deal with Wicca, p. 46.)
Note the seemingly automatic verbal formula at the end of her statement which in reality makes no difference in her decision to cast her spell. She wants the part for herself and if someone else is harmed by losing it ... oh well. This is an extremely minor example but the truth is that Wicca caters to selfishness. When we always choose only what we want we can become very self-centered and self-serving. Those attitudes are contrary to the self-sacrificing sort of life Jesus calls Christians to live.
