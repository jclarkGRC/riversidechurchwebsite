History:

One cannot even begin to consider the history of Scientology without first taking a look at its founder, L. Ron Hubbard. That the origin and development of Scientology is due to Hubbard's work is without doubt:

L. Ron Hubbard is the Founder of Scientology. He has described his philosophy in more than 5,000 writings, including dozens of books, and in 3,000 tape recorded lectures. (What is Scientology?, Bridge Publications, Inc., 1993, p. 25.)

These written and spoken words of Hubbard's are taken by his adherents as the ultimate source of authority for Scientologists. Indeed, as one continues to read various Scientology sources, it becomes abundantly clear that Hubbard is immensely admired by his followers. Words and phrases such as "universal acclaim," "one of the leading lights" and "true Renaissance man" are used by them to describe his character, abilities and popularity. With such a strong connection, then, between the founder and the religion, it is reasonable to expect that an examination of the man himself should provide valuable insight into the movement he started and promoted.

I think the reader will find this relatively brief look at Hubbard to be quite interesting. As we will see, though there may be sharp disagreement between his supporters and his critics about many of the details of his life, none will dispute that he was a very colorful character indeed.

General consensus

Let us begin with those facts about Hubbard with which there is little, if any, dispute between Scientologists, critics and whatever official documentation is available to be cited. Lafayette Ronald Hubbard was born March 13, 1911 in Tilden, Nebraska to Harry Ross and Ledora May Hubbard. By the time L. Ron was two years old, the family had moved to Kalispell, Montana and soon afterward to Helena. In 1917, his father, Harry, rejoined the U.S. Navy as an officer. Public school records show that L. Ron was educated in Montana, California, Washington and Virginia. Apparently the family moved from Montana to California and spent some time there before relocating to the Seattle area in 1923. From 1930-32, he attended George Washington University.

Shortly afterward and for the balance of the 1930's, L. Ron Hubbard began writing for "pulp fiction" magazines and was successful, being best known for his science fiction stories. From 1942-45, he served in the U.S. Navy. In 1950, Hubbard published Dianetics: The Modern Science of Mental Health. The book was a national bestseller and L. Ron founded the Hubbard Dianetic Research Foundation to teach and apply the healing techniques described therein. His second work, Science of Survival, was published in 1951. However, the Foundation did not survive for very long and in its place L. Ron founded Hubbard College which, in 1952, was renamed the Hubbard Association of Scientologists International. In 1954, this organization was absorbed by the newly organized Church of Scientology.

He stepped down as the official leader of the church on September 1, 1966. The church, however, had purchased some large, ocean going vessels and from 1967 to 1975, Hubbard lived mostly at sea developing new techniques for treatments and organizational principles for church administration. Upon his return to shore, he shared and implemented these new ideas and began writing again -- pamphlets and booklets for Scientology and also science fiction novels. He settled in California, first near Palm Springs until 1979 and then near San Luis Obispo until his death on January 24, 1986.

Remarkable claims

Scientologists, however, claim much more for Hubbard than what has thus far been stated. The following informational nuggets come from the previously referenced 1993 edition of their introduction manual and primer What is Scientology? Remarkable as he was, if all we are about to see were also true, L. Ron would certainly have been quite a guy! Consider the following assertions:

Not only could he ride horses at the age of three and a half, but was soon able to rope and break broncos with the best of them. (p. 26)
Under [his mother's] guidance, Ron was reading and writing at an early age, and soon satisfying his insatiable curiosity about life with the works of Shakespeare, the Greek philosophers and other classics. (p. 27)
At the age of six, he became a blood brother of the Blackfeet, an honor bestowed on few white men. (p. 28)
In early 1923, when Ron was twelve, he and his family moved to Seattle, Washington ... He joined the Boy Scouts and that year proudly achieved the rank of Boy Scout First Class. The next year he became the youngest Eagle Scout ever, an early indication that he did not plan to live an ordinary life. (p. 29)
At the end of that year, young Ron traveled to the nation's capital ... meeting Commander Joseph C. Thompson of the US Navy Medical Corps ... [who] was the first officer sent ... to study under Sigmund Freud and took it upon himself to pass on the essentials of Freudian theory to his young friend. (p. 29)
In 1927, at the age of sixteen, Ron took the first of his several voyages across the Pacific to Asia. There, both on his own and in the company of an officer attached to the British legation, he took advantage of this unique opportunity to study Far Eastern culture. Among others he befriended and learned from was a thoroughly insightful Beijing magician who represented the last of the line of Chinese magicians from the court of Kublai Khan. (p. 30)
It was also through the course of these travels that Ron gained access to the much talked about but rarely seen Buddhist lamaseries in the Western Hills of China -- temples usually off limits to both local peasants and visiting foreigners. ... By the age of nineteen, long before the advent of commercial airplane or jet transportation, he had traveled more than a quarter million miles, including voyages not only to China but also Japan, Guam, the Philippines and other points in the Orient. (pp. 31-32)
He enrolled at George Washington University. ... But fate and his father placed him, fortunately in mathematics and engineering instead. With his knowledge of many cultures and his growing awareness of the human condition, his background in engineering and mathematics would serve him well in undertaking a scientific approach to solving the riddles of existence and man's spiritual potential. ... he enrolled in one of the first nuclear physics courses taught in the United States. (p. 35)
Deciding that formal study had nothing more to offer, L. Ron Hubbard left college in the depths of the Depression, ... He directed two expeditions, the Caribbean Motion Picture Expedition, a two-and-a-half month, 5000 mile voyage ... and the West Indies Mineralogical Expedition, which completed the first mineralogical survey of the island of Puerto Rico under US rule. (p. 38)
At the outbreak of World War II, Mr. Hubbard was commissioned as a lieutenant (junior grade) in the US Navy and served as a commander of corvettes. He saw action in both the Atlantic and Pacific, ... In 1945, left partially blind with injured optic nerves and lame from hip and back injuries, Mr. Hubbard was hospitalized at Oak Knoll Naval Hospital in Oakland, California. [After helping others with his new techniques] ... he used the same procedures to cure injuries and wounds he himself had received, fully recovering his health by 1949. So complete was his recovery, ... the Navy Retiring Board reviewing Lt. Hubbard's case ... designated him fit for active duty. (pp. 42-44)

There are more items and claims about Hubbard which are mentioned in other works or in his personal interviews but we will be content to examine these for now. Just how accurate are these various nuggets of information about L. Ron Hubbard, Founder of Scientology? Let's see.

Did L. Ron lie about his life?

On a webpage entitled "What Scientology Won't Tell You" in an article called Who Was L. Ron Hubbard? the author comments that:

The church has published many biographical summaries over the years. These have contained a large number of claims that do not survive fact checking. (xenu.net cited February 23, 2011.)

As we evaluate the information we have seen about Hubbard, obviously, not all the assertions can be either verified or refuted by actual records, especially in his very early years. But this author will contend that, in these cases, common sense can be brought to bear to ascertain the likelihood that they actually occurred. For instance, I could certainly see a three and a half year old boy being placed on a gentle horse and led around by an adult and so, in that way, it can be said that he is riding it. But I seriously doubt that the same boy (or even a four or five year old) is likely to be breaking wild horses. In the same manner, I have heard of precocious children who read the newspaper at four to six years of age but I find it hard to credit that even an extremely bright boy of that age would find the works of Shakespeare, Aristotle, Socrates, etc. to be very interesting or intelligible.

As far as Hubbard being a blood brother of the Blackfeet it hardly seems likely since the reservation was over one hundred miles from his home in Helena. In his book, A Piece of Blue Sky (p. 48), former Scientologist Jon Atack says that at some point in the 1930's, Hubbard admitted that what he knew of the Blackfeet nation came second hand from an acquaintance who actually had been a tribal blood brother. (As cited in The Ultimate Spin Doctor: L. Ron Hubbard -- The Man and His Myth by Craig Branch, The Watchman Expositor.)

The Boy Scouts do have a record of Ronald Hubbard becoming an Eagle Scout in 1924 but it was in Washington, D.C., not Seattle. Further, there is no way to verify he was the youngest ever in history since the Boy Scouts did not keep records of the ages of Eagle Scouts in those years (Branch, The Ultimate Spin Doctor). And with regard to learning the basics of Freudian psychology at the age of thirteen from one of his father's colleagues in the Navy that, too, seems a little farfetched. Why would the man even bother and why would a teenager even be interested -- and, by the way, wouldn't young Ron's father be a little concerned that this colleague of his wanted to spend so much time with his son? It doesn't add up.

As we move on into later years of Hubbard's life, there is more documentation available to either substantiate or refute some of these fantastic claims. For instance, concerning his travel in the Orient during his later teenage years, researchers have pieced together a very different account than what Scientology proposes. Craig Branch, researcher/writer for the Watchman Expositor, does a good job of relating the pertinent information in his article, The Ultimate Spin Doctor: L. Ron Hubbard--The Man and His Myth:

Fact: L. Ron Hubbard, accompanied by his mother, took a round-trip to Guam in 1927 to visit his father who was stationed there.
  • The ship spent a brief time in two Chinese ports, visited Hawaii, Yokohama, Shanghai, Hong Kong, and the Philippines. His diary entries show that he was unimpressed with the culture and the people, in fact, his comments were full of contempt.
  • He spent six weeks on Guam (A Piece of Blue Sky, pp. 53-4).
  • He was back in Helena, attending high school from September 6, 1927 to May 11, 1928 (Ibid., p. 55). He failed to graduate due to lack of credits.
  • In July 1928, Hubbard decided to return to his parents in Guam and it was during this period that he visited China with both parents.
    • The ship docked at Tsingtao, the Hubbards traveled to Peking, Cheffoo, Shanghai, and finally Hong Kong.
    • In Peking, young Ron did visit a Buddhist temple. The trip, in its entirety, lasted two months (Ibid., pp. 55-7).
    • His diary said nothing about studying with wise men of the East, or any other spiritual insights. However, it did describe the Lama temples as "very odd and heathenish" (The Los Angeles Times, June 24, 1990, p. A39). The diary reveals Hubbard's intolerance for other cultures and/or races in noting that China's problem was that there were too many "Chinks" (Ibid.).
(watchman.org cited February 23, 2011.)

Records show that Hubbard did indeed attend George Washington University from 1930-32. However his career there was somewhat less than stellar. In his first year he had a D grade average and was placed on probation. In his second (and final) year he received a D in Calculus and also in Electrical and Magnetic Physics. He received an F in Molecular and Atomic Physics and was placed on academic probation during his second year. He left school and did not return after that second year. (What Scientology Won't Tell You -- An Information Pack "Who Was L. Ron Hubbard?" xenu.net cited Feb. 23, 2011; also Craig Branch, "The Ultimate Spin Doctor" watchman.org cited Feb. 23, 2011.)

Citing A Piece of Blue Sky (pp. 62-63) by Jon Atack, Branch shares that the Caribbean Motion Picture Expedition which Hubbard led actually only made it to three of the sixteen planned stops and that no actual film work was accomplished. In Puerto Rico it was discovered that a man named Bela Hubbard had actually done a mineralogical survey in one district in 1923 (back when L. Ron was 12). No one who had expertise in Puerto Rico's geology had ever heard of L. Ron Hubbard. (Ibid.)

With regard to Hubbard's service in the U.S. Navy during World War II let me once again directly quote Craig Branch's article The Ultimate Spin Doctor at www.watchman.org:

Fact: Through the Freedom of Information Act, The Los Angeles Times received Hubbard's actual military, V.A., and medical records.
  • The highest rank Hubbard ever received was Lieutenant senior grade, not Commodore [as claimed by Scientology] which is the rank between Captain and Rear Admiral.
  • Hubbard's service record shows that he never saw action against the enemy, and received only four awards, none for combat or wounds. He was never awarded the Purple Heart (Ibid.; also, military service records on file).
  • Naval records described Hubbard:
    • "By assuming unauthorized authority and attempting to perform duties for which he has no qualification he became the source of much trouble." ...
    • "This officer is not satisfactory for independent duty assignment. He is garrulous and tries to give impressions of his importance. He also seems to think that he has unusual ability in most lines. These characteristics indicate that he will require close supervision for satisfactory performance of any intelligence duty" (Memo from U.S. Naval Attache L.D. Causey to the Commandant, Twelfth Naval District, February 14, 1942).
  • And more than a year later,
    • "Consider this officer lacking in the essential qualities of judgment, leadership and cooperation. He acts without forethought as to probable results." ...
    • "Not considered qualified for command or promotion at this time. Recommend duty on a large vessel where he can be properly supervised" (File # 113392, "Report on the Fitness of Officers," Period from May 29, 1943 to July 7, 1943).
After claiming to have destroyed two enemy submarines, an investigation concluded it didn't happen. Later, L. Ron Hubbard, according to an investigation, disregarded orders and conducted gunnery practice in Mexican territorial waters. He was relieved of command and a letter of admonition was placed in his files. "In Hubbard's defense, Scientology officials accused others of distorting and misrepresenting his military glories. They say the Navy 'covered up' Hubbard's sinking of the submarines" (Los Angeles Times, June 24, 1990; also, military service records on file).

However, it is not just the Navy who disputes Hubbard's claims for his military career. The March 1, 1998 issue of The Boston Herald in an article by Joseph Mallia interviewed Eugene LaMere, who served on the same vessel as Hubbard. The ship in question, the YP-422, was a refitted "freighter armed only with a 3-inch gun and two .30-caliber machine guns". The article quotes LaMere as saying, "The YP-422 never saw combat." (www.rickross.com cited February 23, 2011.)

The last part of the final item in the list of Hubbard's many accomplishments according to What is Scientology? (pp. 42-44) told of L. Ron using his techniques to heal others and himself so that he was completely healthy and the Navy Review Board was forced to declare him fit for duty. Once again Branch comments:

Furthermore, the medical files show that when he was admitted to Oak Knoll he had 20/20 vision, with glasses, and there is no mention of "injured optic nerves." At the time he left the hospital his eyesight was 12/20 in the right eye and 14/20 in the left, with glasses. This coincided with Hubbard's application for a disability pension. Interestingly, his military records show Hubbard stating he "contracted conjunctivitis from exposure to excessive tropical sunlight" (Ibid.).
Fact: In October 1947 Hubbard wrote to the Veteran's Administration asking for psychiatric treatment due to suffering from wartime service. By 1948, Hubbard was able to get a disability award of 40% for his "duodenal ulcer, infection of the eyes, bursitis of the right shoulder and arthritis of multiple joints."
In an August 1951 medical examination, Hubbard complained of the same conditions listed in his disability award, and according to a letter from the Veteran's Administration, he was still receiving the 40% disability compensation in 1973. In fact, he continued to receive his 40% disability check through at least 1980 (The Los Angeles Times, June 24, 1990, p. A38, and records on file). "Hubbard's Sea Org 'Medical Officer,' Kim Douglas, testified in court that while she attended him from 1975 to 1980, he suffered from arthritis, bursitis and coronary trouble, which Dianetics was supposed to alleviate." He wore glasses, in private, the rest of his life (A Piece of Blue Sky, p. 87).

Unfair portrayal by ex-Scientologists?

Scientologists respond to these sorts of attacks in a few different ways. One way, which Craig Branch mentioned regarding the submarine 'cover-up', is by asserting that some of Hubbard's experiences and records were suppressed or altered so as to put him in a bad light and discredit him. (Why the U.S. Navy would do that several years before Hubbard even dreamed up the concept of Dianetics is another question.)

Another way they defend themselves is to assert that some foes of Scientology are being unfair because they dislike the man personally and have a grudge against him. They are simply disgruntled and looking for payback. It is true that many of his harshest critics are former members of the Church of Scientology who claim to have had some terrible experiences or who were greatly disappointed with what they discovered there. On the other hand, it must also be asked: Who would be more qualified to disclose what is truly happening than those who were on the inside?

One such insider was a man named Gerald Armstrong. Citing once again Jon Atack's book entitled A Piece of Blue Sky (pp. 328-29), Craig Branch shares that Armstrong had been a deeply devoted member of the branch of the Church referred to as Sea Org. This sort of paramilitary, Navy-style arm of Scientology, with uniforms, ribbons, et cetera, was conceived by Hubbard during his eight year cruise. Only the most loyal followers could be part of Sea Org (demonstrated by the fact that to join they must sign an agreement obligating them to serve for a billion years). Armstrong was a trusted aide -- trusted so much, in fact, that he was chosen for the prestigious position of "Personal Public Relations Research Officer". This was, in essence, the title for the coordinator of the effort to put together the official biography concerning the life of one L. Ron Hubbard.

In the course of his duties, Armstrong began gathering all the information on Hubbard possible. Some documents were, of course, a matter of public record, but Armstrong was given access to all the pertinent personal documents available as well. These included letters, diaries, medical records and so on. When Armstrong began to realize that the p ublic image which Hubbard and the Church of Scientology were promoting did not match up very well with the information in the documents he was studying, he decided to leave the Church.

Knowing that the church might not simply let him leave without harassment (or worse), Armstrong made copies or kept many of the documents for his own protection. In 1984 the Church of Scientology did indeed bring legal action against Armstrong charging him with stealing their private papers. In the course of the case [Church of Scientology v. Armstrong, No. C420153 California Supreme Court, 1984], the facts about the truth of Hubbard's life came to light. Once again let me refer to Craig Branch's article:

Armstrong demonstrated, through the documents, that contrary to Hubbard's claims, he was not educated in higher mathematics or physics, did not obtain a bachelor of science degree, was not a civil engineer, nor a nuclear physicist, was not in China at age 14, and lied about the time he did spend traveling in Asia, did not study with Lama priests, was never in India, was not crippled and blinded during the war, was not twice pronounced dead, did not cure himself with his discoveries, was not awarded 21 medals and palms, did not see combat, and that Hubbard had lied about many other things to embellish his image. (L. Ron Hubbard: Messiah or Madman? by Croydon and Hubbard, Jr., pp. 220-2 cited in The Ultimate Spin Doctor www.watchman.org

The judge, Paul Breckenridge of the Los Angeles Superior Court, ruled in Gerald Armstrong's favor and issued a scathing statement based on the evidence:

The organization clearly is schizophrenic and paranoid, and this bizarre combination seems to be a reflection of its founder, LRH. The evidence portrays a man who has been virtually a pathological liar when it comes to his history, background and achievements.

There is no doubt that anytime a movement or organization is criticized, the tendency is to point out the motives and/or the agenda of the critic. In this case those who are yet members of Scientology dismiss critics as being disenchanted or disappointed about their experiences and they question who was truly at fault -- the Church itself or the bitter person? In some cases, there may be some validity to those kinds of responses from the ones being criticized. Certainly it is reasonable to examine the motives and agenda of someone when they launch an assault against another. However, that reasoning does not completely explain the discrepancies between what Hubbard claimed for his life and the cold, hard facts of what actually happened. It would be h ard for me to put my faith in a system developed by a man proven in a court of law to be a "pathological liar".

Intimidation tactics

Gerald Armstrong had good reason to stash documents and information about Hubbard and Scientology -- he knew the organization's methodology. They do not easily let people leave their ranks and they do not allow foes to criticize them with no consequences. Scientologists respond to attacks by critics by counterattacking with the intention of ruining the lives of their enemies. A certain magazine (whose label I do not care to share) interviewed Hubbard's son, L. Ron Hubbard, Jr. which was published in June, 1983. In response to a question about the relative scarcity of news and investigative reporting about Scientology, Hubbard, Jr. replied:

It's very simple. Scientology has always had a "fair-game doctrine" -- a policy of doing absolutely anything to stop an investigation or publication of a critical article in a magazine or newspaper. They have run some incredible operations on the several people who have tried to write books about Scientology. It was almost like a terror campaign. First they'd try throwing every possible lawsuit at the reporter or newspaper. We had a team of attorneys to do just that. The goal was to destroy the enemy. So the solution was always to attack, full-bore, with every possible resource, from every angle, instantaneously it can certainly be overwhelming. A guy would get slapped with twenty-seven lawsuits, and our lawyers would start depositioning absolutely anybody who ever knew the man, digging up dirt while at the same time putting together an operation that would get him into further trouble. I know of one case, concerning Paulette Cooper, who wrote a book called The Scandal of Scientology, in which they spent almost $500,000 trying to destroy her. ...
My father drilled into all of us: Don't go to court thinking to win a lawsuit. You go to court to harass, to delay, to exhaust the enemy financially, physically, mentally. You file every motion you can think of and you just lock them up in court. The courts, for my father, were never used to seek justice or redress, but to destroy the people he thought were enemies, to prevent negative stories from appearing. ...
You must realize that there is very little actual courage in this world. It's pretty easy to bend people around. It doesn't take much to shut people up, it really doesn't. In the fifties all I had to do was call a guy up on the telephone and say, "Well, I think your wife would like to know about your mistress." The response would be a shocked [exclamation]. I'd say, "Well, nobody really wants to divulge that kind of information. I think it would be absolutely terrible if your wife found out, so I'm going to make absolutely sure that she doesn't find out. Now, if you just drop in here for a little more auditing ... Now you know in your heart that the critical things you've been saying about Scientology are just vindictive. They're not really true in your heart. You know that, don't you?" And the guy says. "Yeah, sure, I sure do know that!"
And then, if Scientologists couldn't blackmail you, they'd create some dirt on you through their "special operations." There were quite a few of those operations. This one, for example, happened recently. I wasn't involved in it, but Scientologists tried to get an assistant attorney general of the state of California embroiled in a fake operation where a Scientologist pretended to be a nun and pretended to get pregnant by him and filed papers against him. Then in another scheme they tried to set up the mayor of Clearwater, Florida, for a fake hit-and-run accident. I could give you operation after operation that they set up like this." ...
You see, these people didn't realize where their information was going. They always thought that in Scientology auditing they had the priest-confessor's confidentiality -- but it was never that way. People just assumed it, and still do. But everybody knew what was in everybody's files. ...
I began to see that my father was a sick, sadistic, vicious man. I saw more and more parallels between his behavior and what I read about the way Hitler thought and acted. I was realizing that my father really wanted to destroy his enemies and take over the world. Whoever was perceived as his enemy had to be destroyed, including me. This "fair game" policy since the beginning. The organization couldn't exist without it. It keeps people very quiet. ...
Well, he didn't really want people killed, because how could you really destroy them if you just killed them? What he wanted to do was to destroy their lives, their families, their reputations, their jobs, their money, everything. My father was the type of person who, when it came to destruction, wanted to keep you alive for as long as possible, to torture you, punish you. If he chose to destroy you, he would love to see you lying in the gutter, strung out on booze and drugs, rolling in your own vomit, with your wife and children gone forever: no job, no money. He'd enjoy walking by and kicking you and saying to other people, "Look what I did to this man!" He's the kind of man who would pull the wings off flies and watch them stumble around. You see, this fits in with his Scientology beliefs, also. He felt that if you just died, your spirit would go out and get another body to live in. By destroying an enemy that way, you'd be doing him a favor. You were letting him out from under the thumb of L. Ron. Hubbard, you see?

In response to a question about disenchanted members being able to drop out of Scientology, Hubbard, Jr. said:

There was no way. There were thousands of people, back in the fifties who would come in and receive various levels of training, such as a Hubbard Certified Auditor's Certificate or a Bachelor of Scientology or a Doctorate of Scientology, and if they didn't toe the mark as my father wanted them to, then we would cancel their certificates. And then he would notify the Scientologists in the area where the man lived not to have anything to do with him, to disconnect from him. And if information was available about him, we would spread that information around to his wife, his family, his children, where he worked, everywhere. It was straight blackmail. It was "Stay in the fold or else." Then, later on, they developed what they called an ethics review board. If you didn't toe the mark, you'd be put on trial in front of a kangaroo court and then be sentenced to maybe scrub floors. I heard that you had to walk around with a dirty rag tied around your arm like a badge. You could be made to do anything. You would be locked in a chain locker or handcuffed to a bed. This is in later years. We were simpler in the fifties, more direct. I just went out and beat them up. ...
Yeah. We'd strongarm them. I did it myself. And you had to realize that I weighed around 240 pounds in those days. When I taught Scientology, no students ever blew my courses! I would go out and physically retrieve my students. You know, the Scientologists are now trying to make me out to be the worst person since Attila the Hun. They forget that when I was director of training for the organization, I trained literally thousands of people. I created a lot of the Scientology processes and procedures throughout the fifties. I really helped create and run the organization. I was very deeply involved, very directly, for seven years, during its formulation and building. So I find their attempts to discredit me amusing. I used to have a thing about saying that nobody ever ran out of my courses. If you think est is tough, you ought to have taken courses under me in the fifties! ...
If you signed up for a course and you came to my class, I'd keep you there or go physically retrieve you if you left. ...
I remember locking one girl up in a shack out in the desert for at least a couple of weeks.

Other examples

In the May 6, 1991 edition of Time magazine, an article by Richard Behar entitled The Thriving Cult of Greed and Power (pp. 50-57) was published and concurs with L. Ron Hubbard, Jr.'s testimony:

Scientology devotes vast resources to squelching its critics. Since 1986 Hubbard and his church have been the subject of four unfriendly books, all released by small yet courageous publishers. In each case, the writers have been badgered and heavily sued. One of Hubbard's policies was that all perceived enemies are "fair game" and subject to being "tricked, sued or lied to or destroyed". Those who criticize the church journalists, doctors, lawyers and even judges often find themselves engulfed in litigation, stalked by private eyes, framed for fictional crimes, beaten up or threatened with death. Psychologist Margaret Singer, 69, an outspoken Scientology critic and professor at the University of California, Berkeley, now travels regularly under an assumed name to avoid harassment.
After the Los Angeles Times published a negative series on the church last summer, Scientologists spent an estimated $1 million to plaster the reporters' names on hundreds of billboards and bus placards across the city. Above their names were quotations taken out of context to portray the church in a positive light.
The church's most fearsome advocates are its lawyers. Hubbard warned his followers in writing to "beware of attorneys who tell you not to sue ... the purpose of the suit is to harass and discourage rather than to win." Result: Scientology has brought hundreds of suits against its perceived enemies and today pays an estimated $20 million annually to more than 100 lawyers. One legal goal of Scientology is to bankrupt the opposition or bury it under paper. The church has 71 active lawsuits against the IRS alone. One of them, Miscavige vs. IRS, has required the U.S. to produce an index of 52,000 pages of documents.
Boston attorney Michael Flynn, who helped Scientology victims from 1979 to 1987, personally endured 14 frivolous lawsuits, all of them dismissed. Another lawyer, Joseph Yanny, believes the church "has so subverted justice and the judicial system that it should be barred from seeking equity in any court." He should know: Yanny represented the cult until 1987, when, he says, he was asked to help church officials steal medical records to blackmail an opposing attorney (who was allegedly beaten up instead). Since Yanny quit representing the church, he has been the target of death threats, burglaries, lawsuits and other harassment. ...
Sometimes even the church's biggest zealots can use a little protection. Screen star Travolta, 37, has long served as an unofficial Scientology spokesman, even though he told a magazine in 1983 that he was opposed to the church's management. High-level defectors claim that Travolta has long feared that if he defected, details of his sexual life would be made public. "He felt pretty intimidated about this getting out and told me so," recalls William Franks, the church's former chairman of the board. "There were no outright threats made, but it was implicit. If you leave, they immediately start digging up everything." Franks was driven out in 1981 after attempting to reform the church.
NOTE: Time itself was sued by Scientology for publishing this article but in January, 2001 prevailed in court and had the charges of libel dismissed.

These kinds of hardball methods seem outrageous and bizarre to those outside Scientology. However, it seems such actions are the norm (or at least not uncommon) for those inside the organization. The following accounts come chiefly from Craig Branch's comprehensive series of articles found at www.watchman.org (cited May 4, 2011)

Two recent and protracted cases clearly demonstrate how Scientology assiduously carries out the policies initiated by L. Ron Hubbard for intimidating and harassing its "enemies". Scientology's target in the first case was a former Scientologist, Larry Wollersheim. In 1986, Wollersheim won a $30 million jury verdict in compensatory and punitive damages against the Church of Scientology California mother church in a case that began in 1980. This was later reduced on appeal to $2.5 million. The courts found that Scientology was guilty of intentional and negligent infliction of severe emotional harm. They wrote "Any one of these acts [against Wollersheim] exceeds the 'bounds usually tolerated by a decent society' so as to constitute outrageous conduct ... the Church's actions unquestionably constituted reckless disregard for the likelihood of causing emotional distress. The policy of fair game, by its nature, was intended to punish the person who dared to leave the Church. Here, the church actively encouraged its members to destroy Wollersheim's business. Further, by physically restraining Wollersheim from leaving the Church's ship, and subjecting him to further auditing despite his protests, the Church ignored Wollersheim's emotional state and callously compelled him to continue in a practice k nown to cause him emotional distress (Daily Appellate Report, July 1, 1989, p. 9270). ...
Scientology's target in the second case was David Mayo, also a former Scientologist. Mayo won big in the U.S. 9th Circuit Court of Appeals. Mayo was suing Scientology and in typical fashion, Scientology sued him back. The court dismissed Scientology's suit and awarded Mayo $2.9 million in attorney's fees. The court applied the tough sanctions against Scientology saying the Church had been playing "fast and loose with the legal system" by filing countless frivolous motions, employing "evasions, misrepresentations, broken promises and lies, destruction and concealment of documents (Los Angeles Daily Journal, April 18, 1996, p. 3)

Following Hubbard's directive of, "If possible, of course, ruin him utterly," Scientologists smeared Cooper's reputation, then framed her for a felony. Using stationary she had touched, which therefore contained her fingerprints, they forged a bomb threat against the Church. Upon discovering the plot, called "Operation Freakout," the prosecutors dropped all charges against Cooper. Besides the emotional anguish and disruption of her life, Scientology's outrageous, fraudulent persecution of her cost Cooper $26,000 in legal and psychiatric fees. (Los Angeles Times, June 24, 1990, A39). ...
Another such "attack and destroy" campaign uncovered by the FBI raid was carried out against the then mayor of Clearwater, Florida, Gabe Cazares. Documents revealed that "Scientology agents staged a fake hit-and-run accident" against Cazares (Washington Post, April 28, 1978, p. A-1). Furthermore, Scientology attorney Merrell Vanier persuaded Cazares to use his services in a lawsuit against Scientology. From this undercover position Vanier was able to provide inside information to Scientology, as well as gain access to sensitive files in the State Attorney General's office "which was conducting an investigation of the local Scientology organization." One tactic most often used against those who dare to expose abuse in Scientology is a pattern of slanderous information distribution in the critic's neighborhood or workplace. But some of the most chilling are the stories of personal abuse ncluding people being held against their will unless they complied with more Scientology processing, or being told that they would commit suicide or harm their children if they didn't take more expensive Scientology courses etc. (Gadsden Times, May 4, 1991, pp. A1, A6; Cherokee County Herald, December 12, 1990; personal testimonies on file).
Respected British biographer, Russell Miller, wrote a quite revealing book, Bare Faced Messiah: The True Story of L. Ron Hubbard. Following Hubbard's attack policies, Scientology not only sued him in Britain and the United States but hired their chief "private investigator," Eugene Ingram, to discredit him. Miller soon found himself being questioned by the police as a suspect for a murder because of "an anonymous tip-off from someone who used an extensive knowledge of Miller's work and private life to try to frame him" (Sunday Times, October 25, 1987, p. 3.)
Scientology has a history of using private investigators to pursue and harass critics. Eugene Ingram, fired from the Los Angeles Police Department in 1981, is one of the most notorious. Warrants for Ingram's arrest are still outstanding in Florida and Oklahoma, for impersonating a police officer, and for carrying a concealed weapon (copies of warrants on file at WFI). ...
Many out-of-court settlements have been paid to plaintiffs and defendants in cases involving Scientology. In most of the settlements the parties are barred from disclosing the amount. It has been revealed, however, that Julie Titchbourne received $100,000, Gerald Armstrong received $800,000. Court documents in a U.S. district court also revealed that Scientology had been willing to pay $650,000 to four of at least 10 plaintiffs who all later settled for an undisclosed amount. ...
The church also "settled four multi-million dollar suits" as well as with 11 other plaintiffs and "others with claims against the church" for $2.8 million in 1986 (William Horne, "The Two Faces of Scientology," American Lawyer, July, 1992, p. 5, Cult Awareness Network News, March, 1987, p. 4).
Watchman has on file many published accounts of intense harassment and abuse of former members, media reporters, television companies, newspapers and magazines, lawyers, and even trial judges who had anything to do with either criticizing or bringing Scientology to justice. Two American Lawyer articles detail the extraordinary and chilling accounts of consistent harassment and acts of intimidating experienced by various judges assigned to significant lawsuits against Scientology ("Scientology's War Against Judges," December, 1980; "Two Faces of Scientology," July/August, 1992)."

Occultic connections

There is one last area that we should explore about L. Ron Hubbard before we leave him. He was heavily involved with the occult for several years and it seems that his fascination with it influenced him for the rest of his life. As we will see, in some ways Scientology's philosophies are very compatible with those of Satanism and black magic.

According to his son, L. Ron Hubbard, Jr., when the elder Hubbard was in his mid-teens and living in Washington, D.C. with his parents, his mother took him to the Library of Congress where he obtained a copy of The Book of the Law by Aleister Crowley in which is a manifesto for humanistic philosophy. One of the notable and defining statements of the book is: "Do what thy wilt shall be the whole of the Law." Crowley was a renowned Satanist who referred to himself as "the Beast". The information and worldview fascinated Hubbard and would come out later in his own writings and in the evolution of Scientology (e.g., in a taped lecture from 1952 Hubbard states, "Our whole activity tends to make an individual completely independent of any limitation ..."). (Corydon and Hubbard, Jr., L. Ron Hubbard: Messiah or Madman?, 1987)

But his real involvement did not occur until several years later when, just after the war and his service in the Navy, Hubbard meets and becomes friends with John (Jack) Parsons. Most of the following detail comes from Russell Miller's book Bare-Faced Messiah: The True Story of L. Ron Hubbard. Parsons was a brilliant young rocket scientist working at the California Institute of Technology who had inherited and lived in a large house in Pasadena, California. He was well-known and respected in his field but he led a double life. He and his first wife were members of the occultic society Ordo Templi Orientis (O.T.O.) founded (coincidentally) by Crowley to foster the growth and practice of sensual black magic. As a matter of fact Parsons corresponded with Crowley regularly for the purpose of extending his admiration for the man and seeking his counsel. Parsons turned his house into a boarding house of sorts which was only open to those who would appreciate a less traditional style of life. People of that stripe came and went freely, some more or less made it their home. All the while, various occultic rituals and activities were common. Eventually, Parsons' wife ran off with one of the other members of the O.T.O. and he turned his attention to her sister who soon moved into the house.

Parsons also had a love for science fiction and attended the local meetings of some of their societies and so came to meet many in that field. So it was that, in December, 1945, an artist who did illustrations for such magazines showed up at Parson's house with L. Ron Hubbard tagging along. Parsons and Hubbard immediately hit it off and became close friends. Parsons even risked sharing the secrets of the O.T.O. in order to enlist Hubbard as his 'magical partner'. The following comes from a letter Parsons wrote to Crowley about Hubbard:

About three months ago I met Captain L. Ron Hubbard, a writer and explorer of whom I had known for some time ... He is a gentleman; he has red hair, green eyes, is honest and intelligent, and we have become great friends. He moved in with me about two months ago, and although Betty and I are still friendly, she has transferred her affection to Ron.
Although he has no formal training in Magick, he has an extraordinary amount of experience and understanding in the field. From some of his experiences I deduced that he is in direct touch with some higher intelligence, possibly his Guardian Angel. He describes his Angel as a beautiful winged woman with red hair whom he calls the Empress and who has guided him through his life and saved him many times. He is the most Thelemic [ed. self-willed] person I have ever met and is in complete accord with our own principles ... I think I have made a great gain and as Betty and I are the best of friends, there is little loss. I cared for her rather deeply but I have no desire to control her emotions, and I can, I hope, control my own. I need a magical partner. I have many experiments in mind ... (Miller, Bare-Faced Messiah, p. 118.)

The most outrageous 'experiment' they tried was to try to produce a 'Moon Child' (essentially a child of Satan) by enlisting the aid of a willing woman and employing black magic practices before, during and after the activity. The partnership eventually failed. Hubbard ran off with Parsons' girlfriend (the ex-wife's sister) and about $10,000 of his money. Parsons had to pursue them to Florida to recover part of his money. Eventually Parsons married the woman with whom he tried to create the Moon Child and sold the large house. But it was not long before he was killed by an explosion caused by the mishandling of nitroglycerin in his home laboratory. Some have questioned whether it was an accident or suicide. In the meantime Hubbard married the girl, Sara Northrup, he ran off with but did so before divorcing his first wife, Polly.

Of course, when this information came to light, it did not reflect well on Scientology. In December, 1969, the Church of Scientology released the following:

Hubbard broke up black magic in America ... because he was well known as a writer and philosopher and had friends among the physicists, he was sent in to handle the situation [of black magic being practised in a house in Pasadena occupied by nuclear physicists]. He went to live at the house and investigated the black magic rites and the general situation and found them very bad ... Hubbard's mission was successful far beyond anyone's expectations. The house was torn down. Hubbard rescued a girl they were using. The black magic group was dispersed and never recovered.

Current leadership

L. Ron Hubbard ostensibly stepped down from 'official' leadership of the Church of Scientology in 1966 before going on his eight-year cruise around the world. Of course, there is little doubt he was still in charge and called the shots. After his return to land (southern California to be precise) in 1975 and even though he spent the last five years of his life in self-imposed seclusion (most believe he did this because his health was so bad it was a visible testimony to the ineffectiveness of Scientology and the Dianetic process) he still controlled the organization. So when he died in early 1986 it left a huge void in the Scientology leadership hierarchy.

Into this gap stepped twenty-six year old David Miscavige. He was a high school drop-out and second generation Scientologist who at sixteen became intensely involved in the organization. He worked his way up the leadership ladder and eventually worked closely with Hubbard and was placed over most of the day-to- day operations. In 1986 he was named Chairman of the Board and continues in that capacity today. We do not need to spend much time examining his leadership in detail because Miscavige's leadership style is mostly more of the same brutish, heavy-handed techniques he learned from his mentor, L. Ron Hubbard.

It is worth noting, however, that although probably a majority in the organization continue to be intimidated and silent, there are some brave souls coming forward to tell their stories. This has resulted in many testimonies to the abuse within Scientology and of DM himself (as he likes to be called by his subordinates--or 'COB' or simply 'Sir'). There are multiple allegations of Miscavige personally verbally and physically assaulting those who displease him. This includes profanity-laced tirades, pushing, slapping, punching, slamming into walls or furniture, shoving to the ground and worse. The attacks were to the head and face, to the torso, against men and women alike. People are routinely injured and degraded. It is truly hard to believe such activity can really happen in this day and time in the United States. (For details of such incidents www.xenu-directory.net/scienos/david_miscavige1.html)

One highlight of his tenure we should mention is the restoration of tax-exempt religious status which had been stripped from the 'Church' in 1967. After Scientology sued the IRS (of course) to regain the exemption, the courts ruled in favor of the IRS position in a 1984 U.S. tax court decision and this was later upheld at the U.S. Appeals Court level. The U.S. Supreme Court, after ruling in the IRS's favor in a related case (they disallowed the deducting of the cost of auditing), refused to hear the appealed case in 1992, effectively ending Scientology's recourse. It was curious, then, that in 1993 the IRS reversed itself and restored Scientology's tax-exempt status. Curious because it apparently came about as a result of an unscheduled meeting between Miscavige and the IRS Commissioner Fred Goldberg. Also curious because Goldberg's decision to appoint a committee to negotiate a settlement between the IRS and Scientology bypassed the agency's normal avenues of handling such matters regarding exempt organizations. Not only that but the negotiations and the record of agreements was sealed when other similar cases are open to public examination. Oh, and by coincidence I'm sure, over a hundred lawsuits which had been filed against the IRS by 'individuals' were all dropped immediately after the agreement with Scientology was reached. And by the way, it is also known that private investigators had been retained by Scientology's lawyers to dig into the private lives of IRS officials. Wonder what they found? (The above information comes from: Craig Branch, Scientology: A History of Terror and Abuse, Watchman Expositor, vol. 14, No. 5, 1997 found at www.watchman.org.)
